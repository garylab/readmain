2Years passed. 3The seasons came and went, the short animal lives fled by. 4A time came when there was no one who remembered the old days before the Rebellion, except Clover, Benjamin, Moses the raven, and a number of the pigs.

5Muriel was dead; Bluebell, Jessie, and Pincher were dead. 6Jones too was dead–he had died in an inebriates' home in another part of the country. 7Snowball was forgotten. 8Boxer was forgotten, except by the few who had known him. 9Clover was an old stout mare now, stiff in the joints and with a tendency to rheumy eyes. 10She was two years past the retiring age, but in fact no animal had ever actually retired. 11The talk of setting aside a corner of the pasture for superannuated animals had long since been dropped. 12Napoleon was now a mature boar of twenty-four stone. 13Squealer was so fat that he could with difficulty see out of his eyes. 14Only old Benjamin was much the same as ever, except for being a little greyer about the muzzle, and, since Boxer's death, more morose and taciturn than ever.

15There were many more creatures on the farm now, though the increase was not so great as had been expected in earlier years. 16Many animals had been born to whom the Rebellion was only a dim tradition, passed on by word of mouth, and others had been bought who had never heard mention of such a thing before their arrival. 17The farm possessed three horses now besides Clover. 18They were fine upstanding beasts, willing workers and good comrades, but very stupid. 19None of them proved able to learn the alphabet beyond the letter B. 20They accepted everything that they were told about the Rebellion and the principles of Animalism, especially from Clover, for whom they had an almost filial respect; but it was doubtful whether they understood very much of it.

21The farm was more prosperous now, and better organised: it had even been enlarged by two fields which had been bought from Mr. Pilkington. 22The windmill had been successfully completed at last, and the farm possessed a threshing machine and a hay elevator of its own, and various new buildings had been added to it. 23Whymper had bought himself a dogcart. 24The windmill, however, had not after all been used for generating electrical power. 25It was used for milling corn, and brought in a handsome money profit. 26The animals were hard at work building yet another windmill; when that one was finished, so it was said, the dynamos would be installed. 27But the luxuries of which Snowball had once taught the animals to dream, the stalls with electric light and hot and cold water, and the three-day week, were no longer talked about. 28Napoleon had denounced such ideas as contrary to the spirit of Animalism. 29The truest happiness, he said, lay in working hard and living frugally.

30Somehow it seemed as though the farm had grown richer without making the animals themselves any richer–except, of course, for the pigs and the dogs. 31Perhaps this was partly because there were so many pigs and so many dogs. 32It was not that these creatures did not work, after their fashion. 33There was, as Squealer was never tired of explaining, endless work in the supervision and organisation of the farm. 34Much of this work was of a kind that the other animals were too ignorant to understand. 35For example, Squealer told them that the pigs had to expend enormous labours every day upon mysterious things called "files," "reports," "minutes," and "memoranda." 36These were large sheets of paper which had to be closely covered with writing, and as soon as they were so covered, they were burnt in the furnace. 37This was of the highest importance for the welfare of the farm, Squealer said. 38But still, neither pigs nor dogs produced any food by their own labour; and there were very many of them, and their appetites were always good.

39As for the others, their life, so far as they knew, was as it had always been. 40They were generally hungry, they slept on straw, they drank from the pool, they laboured in the fields; in winter they were troubled by the cold, and in summer by the flies. 41Sometimes the older ones among them racked their dim memories and tried to determine whether in the early days of the Rebellion, when Jones's expulsion was still recent, things had been better or worse than now. 42They could not remember. 43There was nothing with which they could compare their present lives: they had nothing to go upon except Squealer's lists of figures, which invariably demonstrated that everything was getting better and better. 44The animals found the problem insoluble; in any case, they had little time for speculating on such things now. 45Only old Benjamin professed to remember every detail of his long life and to know that things never had been, nor ever could be much better or much worse–hunger, hardship, and disappointment being, so he said, the unalterable law of life.

46And yet the animals never gave up hope. 47More, they never lost, even for an instant, their sense of honour and privilege in being members of Animal Farm. 48They were still the only farm in the whole county–in all England!–owned and operated by animals. 49Not one of them, not even the youngest, not even the newcomers who had been brought from farms ten or twenty miles away, ever ceased to marvel at that. 50And when they heard the gun booming and saw the green flag fluttering at the masthead, their hearts swelled with imperishable pride, and the talk turned always towards the old heroic days, the expulsion of Jones, the writing of the Seven Commandments, the great battles in which the human invaders had been defeated. 51None of the old dreams had been abandoned. 52The Republic of the Animals which Major had foretold, when the green fields of England should be untrodden by human feet, was still believed in. 53Some day it was coming: it might not be soon, it might not be with in the lifetime of any animal now living, but still it was coming. 54Even the tune of Beasts of England was perhaps hummed secretly here and there: at any rate, it was a fact that every animal on the farm knew it, though no one would have dared to sing it aloud. 55It might be that their lives were hard and that not all of their hopes had been fulfilled; but they were conscious that they were not as other animals. 56If they went hungry, it was not from feeding tyrannical human beings; if they worked hard, at least they worked for themselves. 57No creature among them went upon two legs. 58No creature called any other creature "Master." 59All animals were equal.

60One day in early summer Squealer ordered the sheep to follow him, and led them out to a piece of waste ground at the other end of the farm, which had become overgrown with birch saplings. 61The sheep spent the whole day there browsing at the leaves under Squealer's supervision. 62In the evening he returned to the farmhouse himself, but, as it was warm weather, told the sheep to stay where they were. 63It ended by their remaining there for a whole week, during which time the other animals saw nothing of them. 64Squealer was with them for the greater part of every day. 65He was, he said, teaching them to sing a new song, for which privacy was needed.

66It was just after the sheep had returned, on a pleasant evening when the animals had finished work and were making their way back to the farm buildings, that the terrified neighing of a horse sounded from the yard. 67Startled, the animals stopped in their tracks. 68It was Clover's voice. 69She neighed again, and all the animals broke into a gallop and rushed into the yard. 70Then they saw what Clover had seen.

71It was a pig walking on his hind legs.

72Yes, it was Squealer. 73A little awkwardly, as though not quite used to supporting his considerable bulk in that position, but with perfect balance, he was strolling across the yard. 74And a moment later, out from the door of the farmhouse came a long file of pigs, all walking on their hind legs. 75Some did it better than others, one or two were even a trifle unsteady and looked as though they would have liked the support of a stick, but every one of them made his way right round the yard successfully. 76And finally there was a tremendous baying of dogs and a shrill crowing from the black cockerel, and out came Napoleon himself, majestically upright, casting haughty glances from side to side, and with his dogs gambolling round him.

77He carried a whip in his trotter.

78There was a deadly silence. 79Amazed, terrified, huddling together, the animals watched the long line of pigs march slowly round the yard. 80It was as though the world had turned upside-down. 81Then there came a moment when the first shock had worn off and when, in spite of everything–in spite of their terror of the dogs, and of the habit, developed through long years, of never complaining, never criticising, no matter what happened–they might have uttered some word of protest. 82But just at that moment, as though at a signal, all the sheep burst out into a tremendous bleating of–

83"Four legs good, two legs better! 84Four legs good, two legs better! 85Four legs good, two legs better!"

86It went on for five minutes without stopping. 87And by the time the sheep had quieted down, the chance to utter any protest had passed, for the pigs had marched back into the farmhouse.

88Benjamin felt a nose nuzzling at his shoulder. 89He looked round. 90It was Clover. 91Her old eyes looked dimmer than ever. 92Without saying anything, she tugged gently at his mane and led him round to the end of the big barn, where the Seven Commandments were written. 93For a minute or two they stood gazing at the tatted wall with its white lettering.

94"My sight is failing," she said finally. 95"Even when I was young I could not have read what was written there. 96But it appears to me that that wall looks different. 97Are the Seven Commandments the same as they used to be, Benjamin?"

98For once Benjamin consented to break his rule, and he read out to her what was written on the wall. 99There was nothing there now except a single Commandment. 100It ran:

101ALL ANIMALS ARE EQUAL

102BUT SOME ANIMALS ARE MORE EQUAL THAN OTHERS

103After that it did not seem strange when next day the pigs who were supervising the work of the farm all carried whips in their trotters. 104It did not seem strange to learn that the pigs had bought themselves a wireless set, were arranging to install a telephone, and had taken out subscriptions to John Bull, TitBits, and the Daily Mirror. 105It did not seem strange when Napoleon was seen strolling in the farmhouse garden with a pipe in his mouth–no, not even when the pigs took Mr. Jones's clothes out of the wardrobes and put them on, Napoleon himself appearing in a black coat, ratcatcher breeches, and leather leggings, while his favourite sow appeared in the watered silk dress which Mrs. Jones had been used to wear on Sundays.

106A week later, in the afternoon, a number of dogcarts drove up to the farm. 107A deputation of neighbouring farmers had been invited to make a tour of inspection. 108They were shown all over the farm, and expressed great admiration for everything they saw, especially the windmill. 109The animals were weeding the turnip field. 110They worked diligently, hardly raising their faces from the ground, and not knowing whether to be more frightened of the pigs or of the human visitors.

111That evening loud laughter and bursts of singing came from the farmhouse. 112And suddenly, at the sound of the mingled voices, the animals were stricken with curiosity. 113What could be happening in there, now that for the first time animals and human beings were meeting on terms of equality? 114With one accord they began to creep as quietly as possible into the farmhouse garden.

115At the gate they paused, half frightened to go on but Clover led the way in. 116They tiptoed up to the house, and such animals as were tall enough peered in at the dining-room window. 117There, round the long table, sat half a dozen farmers and half a dozen of the more eminent pigs, Napoleon himself occupying the seat of honour at the head of the table. 118The pigs appeared completely at ease in their chairs The company had been enjoying a game of cards but had broken off for the moment, evidently in order to drink a toast. 119A large jug was circulating, and the mugs were being refilled with beer. 120No one noticed the wondering faces of the animals that gazed in at the window.

121Mr. Pilkington, of Foxwood, had stood up, his mug in his hand. 122In a moment, he said, he would ask the present company to drink a toast. 123But before doing so, there were a few words that he felt it incumbent upon him to say.

124It was a source of great satisfaction to him, he said–and, he was sure, to all others present–to feel that a long period of mistrust and misunderstanding had now come to an end. 125There had been a time–not that he, or any of the present company, had shared such sentiments–but there had been a time when the respected proprietors of Animal Farm had been regarded, he would not say with hostility, but perhaps with a certain measure of misgiving, by their human neighbours. 126Unfortunate incidents had occurred, mistaken ideas had been current. 127It had been felt that the existence of a farm owned and operated by pigs was somehow abnormal and was liable to have an unsettling effect in the neighbourhood. 128Too many farmers had assumed, without due enquiry, that on such a farm a spirit of licence and indiscipline would prevail. 129They had been nervous about the effects upon their own animals, or even upon their human employees. 130But all such doubts were now dispelled. 131Today he and his friends had visited Animal Farm and inspected every inch of it with their own eyes, and what did they find? 132Not only the most up-to-date methods, but a discipline and an orderliness which should be an example to all farmers everywhere. 133He believed that he was right in saying that the lower animals on Animal Farm did more work and received less food than any animals in the county. 134Indeed, he and his fellow-visitors today had observed many features which they intended to introduce on their own farms immediately.

135He would end his remarks, he said, by emphasising once again the friendly feelings that subsisted, and ought to subsist, between Animal Farm and its neighbours. 136Between pigs and human beings there was not, and there need not be, any clash of interests whatever. 137Their struggles and their difficulties were one. 138Was not the labour problem the same everywhere? 139Here it became apparent that Mr. Pilkington was about to spring some carefully prepared witticism on the company, but for a moment he was too overcome by amusement to be able to utter it. 140After much choking, during which his various chins turned purple, he managed to get it out: "If you have your lower animals to contend with," he said, "we have our lower classes!" 141This bon mot set the table in a roar; and Mr. Pilkington once again congratulated the pigs on the low rations, the long working hours, and the general absence of pampering which he had observed on Animal Farm.

142And now, he said finally, he would ask the company to rise to their feet and make certain that their glasses were full. 143"Gentlemen," concluded Mr. Pilkington, "gentlemen, I give you a toast: To the prosperity of Animal Farm!"

144There was enthusiastic cheering and stamping of feet. 145Napoleon was so gratified that he left his place and came round the table to clink his mug against Mr. Pilkington's before emptying it. 146When the cheering had died down, Napoleon, who had remained on his feet, intimated that he too had a few words to say.

147Like all of Napoleon's speeches, it was short and to the point. 148He too, he said, was happy that the period of misunderstanding was at an end. 149For a long time there had been rumours–circulated, he had reason to think, by some malignant enemy–that there was something subversive and even revolutionary in the outlook of himself and his colleagues. 150They had been credited with attempting to stir up rebellion among the animals on neighbouring farms. 151Nothing could be further from the truth! 152Their sole wish, now and in the past, was to live at peace and in normal business relations with their neighbours. 153This farm which he had the honour to control, he added, was a co-operative enterprise. 154The title-deeds, which were in his own possession, were owned by the pigs jointly.

155He did not believe, he said, that any of the old suspicions still lingered, but certain changes had been made recently in the routine of the farm which should have the effect of promoting confidence stiff further. 156Hitherto the animals on the farm had had a rather foolish custom of addressing one another as "Comrade." 157This was to be suppressed. 158There had also been a very strange custom, whose origin was unknown, of marching every Sunday morning past a boar's skull which was nailed to a post in the garden. 159This, too, would be suppressed, and the skull had already been buried. 160His visitors might have observed, too, the green flag which flew from the masthead. 161If so, they would perhaps have noted that the white hoof and horn with which it had previously been marked had now been removed. 162It would be a plain green flag from now onwards.

163He had only one criticism, he said, to make of Mr. Pilkington's excellent and neighbourly speech. 164Mr. Pilkington had referred throughout to "Animal Farm." 165He could not of course know–for he, Napoleon, was only now for the first time announcing it–that the name "Animal Farm" had been abolished. 166Henceforward the farm was to be known as "The Manor Farm"–which, he believed, was its correct and original name.

167"Gentlemen," concluded Napoleon, "I will give you the same toast as before, but in a different form. 168Fill your glasses to the brim. 169Gentlemen, here is my toast: To the prosperity of The Manor Farm! "

170There was the same hearty cheering as before, and the mugs were emptied to the dregs. 171But as the animals outside gazed at the scene, it seemed to them that some strange thing was happening. 172What was it that had altered in the faces of the pigs? 173Clover's old dim eyes flitted from one face to another. 174Some of them had five chins, some had four, some had three. 175But what was it that seemed to be melting and changing? 176Then, the applause having come to an end, the company took up their cards and continued the game that had been interrupted, and the animals crept silently away.

177But they had not gone twenty yards when they stopped short. 178An uproar of voices was coming from the farmhouse. 179They rushed back and looked through the window again. 180Yes, a violent quarrel was in progress. 181There were shoutings, bangings on the table, sharp suspicious glances, furious denials. 182The source of the trouble appeared to be that Napoleon and Mr. Pilkington had each played an ace of spades simultaneously.

183Twelve voices were shouting in anger, and they were all alike. 184No question, now, what had happened to the faces of the pigs. 185The creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which.
