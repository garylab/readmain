IX

1After two years I remember the rest of that day, and that night and the next day, only as an endless drill of police and photographers and newspaper men in and out of Gatsby’s front door. 2A rope stretched across the main gate and a policeman by it kept out the curious, but little boys soon discovered that they could enter through my yard, and there were always a few of them clustered open-mouthed about the pool. 3Someone with a positive manner, perhaps a detective, used the expression “madman” as he bent over Wilson’s body that afternoon, and the adventitious authority of his voice set the key for the newspaper reports next morning.

4Most of those reports were a nightmare—grotesque, circumstantial, eager, and untrue. 5When Michaelis’s testimony at the inquest brought to light Wilson’s suspicions of his wife I thought the whole tale would shortly be served up in racy pasquinade—but Catherine, who might have said anything, didn’t say a word. 6She showed a surprising amount of character about it too—looked at the coroner with determined eyes under that corrected brow of hers, and swore that her sister had never seen Gatsby, that her sister was completely happy with her husband, that her sister had been into no mischief whatever. 7She convinced herself of it, and cried into her handkerchief, as if the very suggestion was more than she could endure. 8So Wilson was reduced to a man “deranged by grief” in order that the case might remain in its simplest form. 9And it rested there.

10But all this part of it seemed remote and unessential. 11I found myself on Gatsby’s side, and alone. 12From the moment I telephoned news of the catastrophe to West Egg village, every surmise about him, and every practical question, was referred to me. 13At first I was surprised and confused; then, as he lay in his house and didn’t move or breathe or speak, hour upon hour, it grew upon me that I was responsible, because no one else was interested—interested, I mean, with that intense personal interest to which everyone has some vague right at the end.

14I called up Daisy half an hour after we found him, called her instinctively and without hesitation. 15But she and Tom had gone away early that afternoon, and taken baggage with them.

16“Left no address?”

17“No.”

18“Say when they’d be back?”

19“No.”

20“Any idea where they are? 21How I could reach them?”

22“I don’t know. 23Can’t say.”

24I wanted to get somebody for him. 25I wanted to go into the room where he lay and reassure him: “I’ll get somebody for you, Gatsby. 26Don’t worry. 27Just trust me and I’ll get somebody for you—”

28Meyer Wolfshiem’s name wasn’t in the phone book. 29The butler gave me his office address on Broadway, and I called Information, but by the time I had the number it was long after five, and no one answered the phone.

30“Will you ring again?”

31“I’ve rung three times.”

32“It’s very important.”

33“Sorry. 34I’m afraid no one’s there.”

35I went back to the drawing-room and thought for an instant that they were chance visitors, all these official people who suddenly filled it. 36But, though they drew back the sheet and looked at Gatsby with shocked eyes, his protest continued in my brain:

37“Look here, old sport, you’ve got to get somebody for me. 38You’ve got to try hard. 39I can’t go through this alone.”

40Someone started to ask me questions, but I broke away and going upstairs looked hastily through the unlocked parts of his desk—he’d never told me definitely that his parents were dead. 41But there was nothing—only the picture of Dan Cody, a token of forgotten violence, staring down from the wall.

42Next morning I sent the butler to New York with a letter to Wolfshiem, which asked for information and urged him to come out on the next train. 43That request seemed superfluous when I wrote it. 44I was sure he’d start when he saw the newspapers, just as I was sure there’d be a wire from Daisy before noon—but neither a wire nor Mr. Wolfshiem arrived; no one arrived except more police and photographers and newspaper men. 45When the butler brought back Wolfshiem’s answer I began to have a feeling of defiance, of scornful solidarity between Gatsby and me against them all.

46Dear Mr. Carraway. 47This has been one of the most terrible shocks of my life to me I hardly can believe it that it is true at all. 48Such a mad act as that man did should make us all think. 49I cannot come down now as I am tied up in some very important business and cannot get mixed up in this thing now. 50If there is anything I can do a little later let me know in a letter by Edgar. 51I hardly know where I am when I hear about a thing like this and am completely knocked down and out.

52Yours truly

53Meyer Wolfshiem

54and then hasty addenda beneath:

55Let me know about the funeral etc do not know his family at all.

56When the phone rang that afternoon and Long Distance said Chicago was calling I thought this would be Daisy at last. 57But the connection came through as a man’s voice, very thin and far away.

58“This is Slagle speaking…”

59“Yes?” 60The name was unfamiliar.

61“Hell of a note, isn’t it? 62Get my wire?”

63“There haven’t been any wires.”

64“Young Parke’s in trouble,” he said rapidly. 65“They picked him up when he handed the bonds over the counter. 66They got a circular from New York giving ’em the numbers just five minutes before. 67What d’you know about that, hey? 68You never can tell in these hick towns—”

69“Hello!” 70I interrupted breathlessly. 71“Look here—this isn’t Mr. Gatsby. 72Mr. Gatsby’s dead.”

73There was a long silence on the other end of the wire, followed by an exclamation… then a quick squawk as the connection was broken.

74I think it was on the third day that a telegram signed Henry C. Gatz arrived from a town in Minnesota. 75It said only that the sender was leaving immediately and to postpone the funeral until he came.

76It was Gatsby’s father, a solemn old man, very helpless and dismayed, bundled up in a long cheap ulster against the warm September day. 77His eyes leaked continuously with excitement, and when I took the bag and umbrella from his hands he began to pull so incessantly at his sparse grey beard that I had difficulty in getting off his coat. 78He was on the point of collapse, so I took him into the music-room and made him sit down while I sent for something to eat. 79But he wouldn’t eat, and the glass of milk spilled from his trembling hand.

80“I saw it in the Chicago newspaper,” he said. 81“It was all in the Chicago newspaper. 82I started right away.”

83“I didn’t know how to reach you.”

84His eyes, seeing nothing, moved ceaselessly about the room.

85“It was a madman,” he said. 86“He must have been mad.”

87“Wouldn’t you like some coffee?” 88I urged him.

89“I don’t want anything. 90I’m all right now, Mr.—”

91“Carraway.”

92“Well, I’m all right now. 93Where have they got Jimmy?”

94I took him into the drawing-room, where his son lay, and left him there. 95Some little boys had come up on the steps and were looking into the hall; when I told them who had arrived, they went reluctantly away.

96After a little while Mr. Gatz opened the door and came out, his mouth ajar, his face flushed slightly, his eyes leaking isolated and unpunctual tears. 97He had reached an age where death no longer has the quality of ghastly surprise, and when he looked around him now for the first time and saw the height and splendour of the hall and the great rooms opening out from it into other rooms, his grief began to be mixed with an awed pride. 98I helped him to a bedroom upstairs; while he took off his coat and vest I told him that all arrangements had been deferred until he came.

99“I didn’t know what you’d want, Mr. Gatsby—”

100“Gatz is my name.”

101“—Mr. Gatz. 102I thought you might want to take the body West.”

103He shook his head.

104“Jimmy always liked it better down East. 105He rose up to his position in the East. 106Were you a friend of my boy’s, Mr.—?”

107“We were close friends.”

108“He had a big future before him, you know. 109He was only a young man, but he had a lot of brain power here.”

110He touched his head impressively, and I nodded.

111“If he’d of lived, he’d of been a great man. 112A man like James J. Hill. 113He’d of helped build up the country.”

114“That’s true,” I said, uncomfortably.

115He fumbled at the embroidered coverlet, trying to take it from the bed, and lay down stiffly—was instantly asleep.

116That night an obviously frightened person called up, and demanded to know who I was before he would give his name.

117“This is Mr. Carraway,” I said.

118“Oh!” He sounded relieved. 119“This is Klipspringer.”

120I was relieved too, for that seemed to promise another friend at Gatsby’s grave. 121I didn’t want it to be in the papers and draw a sightseeing crowd, so I’d been calling up a few people myself. 122They were hard to find.

123“The funeral’s tomorrow,” I said. 124“Three o’clock, here at the house. 125I wish you’d tell anybody who’d be interested.”

126“Oh, I will,” he broke out hastily. 127“Of course I’m not likely to see anybody, but if I do.”

128His tone made me suspicious.

129“Of course you’ll be there yourself.”

130“Well, I’ll certainly try. 131What I called up about is—”

132“Wait a minute,” I interrupted. 133“How about saying you’ll come?”

134“Well, the fact is—the truth of the matter is that I’m staying with some people up here in Greenwich, and they rather expect me to be with them tomorrow. 135In fact, there’s a sort of picnic or something. 136Of course I’ll do my best to get away.”

137I ejaculated an unrestrained “Huh!” 138and he must have heard me, for he went on nervously:

139“What I called up about was a pair of shoes I left there. 140I wonder if it’d be too much trouble to have the butler send them on. 141You see, they’re tennis shoes, and I’m sort of helpless without them. 142My address is care of B. F.—”

143I didn’t hear the rest of the name, because I hung up the receiver.

144After that I felt a certain shame for Gatsby—one gentleman to whom I telephoned implied that he had got what he deserved. 145However, that was my fault, for he was one of those who used to sneer most bitterly at Gatsby on the courage of Gatsby’s liquor, and I should have known better than to call him.

146The morning of the funeral I went up to New York to see Meyer Wolfshiem; I couldn’t seem to reach him any other way. 147The door that I pushed open, on the advice of an elevator boy, was marked “The Swastika Holding Company,” and at first there didn’t seem to be anyone inside. 148But when I’d shouted “hello” several times in vain, an argument broke out behind a partition, and presently a lovely Jewess appeared at an interior door and scrutinized me with black hostile eyes.

149“Nobody’s in,” she said. 150“Mr. Wolfshiem’s gone to Chicago.”

151The first part of this was obviously untrue, for someone had begun to whistle “The Rosary,” tunelessly, inside.

152“Please say that Mr. Carraway wants to see him.”

153“I can’t get him back from Chicago, can I?”

154At this moment a voice, unmistakably Wolfshiem’s, called “Stella!” 155from the other side of the door.

156“Leave your name on the desk,” she said quickly. 157“I’ll give it to him when he gets back.”

158“But I know he’s there.”

159She took a step toward me and began to slide her hands indignantly up and down her hips.

160“You young men think you can force your way in here any time,” she scolded. 161“We’re getting sickantired of it. 162When I say he’s in Chicago, he’s in Chicago.”

163I mentioned Gatsby.

164“Oh-h!” 165She looked at me over again. 166“Will you just—What was your name?”

167She vanished. 168In a moment Meyer Wolfshiem stood solemnly in the doorway, holding out both hands. 169He drew me into his office, remarking in a reverent voice that it was a sad time for all of us, and offered me a cigar.

170“My memory goes back to when first I met him,” he said. 171“A young major just out of the army and covered over with medals he got in the war. 172He was so hard up he had to keep on wearing his uniform because he couldn’t buy some regular clothes. 173First time I saw him was when he came into Winebrenner’s poolroom at Forty-third Street and asked for a job. 174He hadn’t eat anything for a couple of days. 175‘Come on have some lunch with me,’ I said. 176He ate more than four dollars’ worth of food in half an hour.”

177“Did you start him in business?” 178I inquired.

179“Start him! 180I made him.”

181“Oh.”

182“I raised him up out of nothing, right out of the gutter. 183I saw right away he was a fine-appearing, gentlemanly young man, and when he told me he was at Oggsford I knew I could use him good. 184I got him to join the American Legion and he used to stand high there. 185Right off he did some work for a client of mine up to Albany. 186We were so thick like that in everything”—he held up two bulbous fingers—“always together.”

187I wondered if this partnership had included the World’s Series transaction in 1919.

188“Now he’s dead,” I said after a moment. 189“You were his closest friend, so I know you’ll want to come to his funeral this afternoon.”

190“I’d like to come.”

191“Well, come then.”

192The hair in his nostrils quivered slightly, and as he shook his head his eyes filled with tears.

193“I can’t do it—I can’t get mixed up in it,” he said.

194“There’s nothing to get mixed up in. 195It’s all over now.”

196“When a man gets killed I never like to get mixed up in it in any way. 197I keep out. 198When I was a young man it was different—if a friend of mine died, no matter how, I stuck with them to the end. 199You may think that’s sentimental, but I mean it—to the bitter end.”

200I saw that for some reason of his own he was determined not to come, so I stood up.

201“Are you a college man?” 202he inquired suddenly.

203For a moment I thought he was going to suggest a “gonnegtion,” but he only nodded and shook my hand.

204“Let us learn to show our friendship for a man when he is alive and not after he is dead,” he suggested. 205“After that my own rule is to let everything alone.”

206When I left his office the sky had turned dark 207and I got back to West Egg in a drizzle. 208After changing my clothes I went next door and found Mr. Gatz walking up and down excitedly in the hall. 209His pride in his son and in his son’s possessions was continually increasing and now he had something to show me.

210“Jimmy sent me this picture.” 211He took out his wallet with trembling fingers. 212“Look there.”

213It was a photograph of the house, cracked in the corners and dirty with many hands. 214He pointed out every detail to me eagerly. 215“Look there!” and then sought admiration from my eyes. 216He had shown it so often that I think it was more real to him now than the house itself.

217“Jimmy sent it to me. 218I think it’s a very pretty picture. 219It shows up well.”

220“Very well. 221Had you seen him lately?”

222“He come out to see me two years ago and bought me the house I live in now. 223Of course we was broke up when he run off from home, but I see now there was a reason for it. 224He knew he had a big future in front of him. 225And ever since he made a success he was very generous with me.”

226He seemed reluctant to put away the picture, held it for another minute, lingeringly, before my eyes. 227Then he returned the wallet and pulled from his pocket a ragged old copy of a book called Hopalong Cassidy.

228“Look here, this is a book he had when he was a boy. 229It just shows you.”

230He opened it at the back cover and turned it around for me to see. 231On the last flyleaf was printed the word schedule, and the date September 12, 1906. 232And underneath:

Dumbell exercise and wall-scaling 6:15⁠–⁠6:30 “

246“I came across this book by accident,” said the old man. 247“It just shows you, don’t it?”

248“It just shows you.”

249“Jimmy was bound to get ahead. 250He always had some resolves like this or something. 251Do you notice what he’s got about improving his mind? 252He was always great for that. 253He told me I et like a hog once, and I beat him for it.”

254He was reluctant to close the book, reading each item aloud and then looking eagerly at me. 255I think he rather expected me to copy down the list for my own use.

256A little before three the Lutheran minister arrived from Flushing, and I began to look involuntarily out the windows for other cars. 257So did Gatsby’s father. 258And as the time passed and the servants came in and stood waiting in the hall, his eyes began to blink anxiously, and he spoke of the rain in a worried, uncertain way. 259The minister glanced several times at his watch, so I took him aside and asked him to wait for half an hour. 260But it wasn’t any use. 261Nobody came.

262About five o’clock our procession of three cars reached the cemetery and stopped in a thick drizzle beside the gate—first a motor hearse, horribly black and wet, then Mr. Gatz and the minister and me in the limousine, and a little later four or five servants and the postman from West Egg, in Gatsby’s station wagon, all wet to the skin. 263As we started through the gate into the cemetery I heard a car stop and then the sound of someone splashing after us over the soggy ground. 264I looked around. 265It was the man with owl-eyed glasses whom I had found marvelling over Gatsby’s books in the library one night three months before.

266I’d never seen him since then. 267I don’t know how he knew about the funeral, or even his name. 268The rain poured down his thick glasses, and he took them off and wiped them to see the protecting canvas unrolled from Gatsby’s grave.

269I tried to think about Gatsby then for a moment, but he was already too far away, and I could only remember, without resentment, that Daisy hadn’t sent a message or a flower. 270Dimly I heard someone murmur “Blessed are the dead that the rain falls on,” and then the owl-eyed man said “Amen to that,” in a brave voice.

271We straggled down quickly through the rain to the cars. 272Owl-eyes spoke to me by the gate.

273“I couldn’t get to the house,” he remarked.

274“Neither could anybody else.”

275“Go on!” 276He started. 277“Why, my God! 278they used to go there by the hundreds.”

279He took off his glasses and wiped them again, outside and in.

280“The poor son-of-a-bitch,” he said.

281One of my most vivid memories is of coming back West from prep school and later from college at Christmas time. 282Those who went farther than Chicago would gather in the old dim Union Station at six o’clock of a December evening, with a few Chicago friends, already caught up into their own holiday gaieties, to bid them a hasty goodbye. 283I remember the fur coats of the girls returning from Miss This-or-That’s and the chatter of frozen breath and the hands waving overhead as we caught sight of old acquaintances, and the matchings of invitations: “Are you going to the Ordways’? 284the Herseys’? 285the Schultzes’?” and the long green tickets clasped tight in our gloved hands. 286And last the murky yellow cars of the Chicago, Milwaukee and St. Paul railroad looking cheerful as Christmas itself on the tracks beside the gate.

287When we pulled out into the winter night and the real snow, our snow, began to stretch out beside us and twinkle against the windows, and the dim lights of small Wisconsin stations moved by, a sharp wild brace came suddenly into the air. 288We drew in deep breaths of it as we walked back from dinner through the cold vestibules, unutterably aware of our identity with this country for one strange hour, before we melted indistinguishably into it again.

289That’s my Middle West—not the wheat or the prairies or the lost Swede towns, but the thrilling returning trains of my youth, and the street lamps and sleigh bells in the frosty dark and the shadows of holly wreaths thrown by lighted windows on the snow. 290I am part of that, a little solemn with the feel of those long winters, a little complacent from growing up in the Carraway house in a city where dwellings are still called through decades by a family’s name. 291I see now that this has been a story of the West, after all—Tom and Gatsby, Daisy and Jordan and I, were all Westerners, and perhaps we possessed some deficiency in common which made us subtly unadaptable to Eastern life.

292Even when the East excited me most, even when I was most keenly aware of its superiority to the bored, sprawling, swollen towns beyond the Ohio, with their interminable inquisitions which spared only the children and the very old—even then it had always for me a quality of distortion. 293West Egg, especially, still figures in my more fantastic dreams. 294I see it as a night scene by El Greco: a hundred houses, at once conventional and grotesque, crouching under a sullen, overhanging sky and a lustreless moon. 295In the foreground four solemn men in dress suits are walking along the sidewalk with a stretcher on which lies a drunken woman in a white evening dress. 296Her hand, which dangles over the side, sparkles cold with jewels. 297Gravely the men turn in at a house—the wrong house. 298But no one knows the woman’s name, and no one cares.

299After Gatsby’s death the East was haunted for me like that, distorted beyond my eyes’ power of correction. 300So when the blue smoke of brittle leaves was in the air and the wind blew the wet laundry stiff on the line I decided to come back home.

301There was one thing to be done before I left, an awkward, unpleasant thing that perhaps had better have been let alone. 302But I wanted to leave things in order and not just trust that obliging and indifferent sea to sweep my refuse away. 303I saw Jordan Baker and talked over and around what had happened to us together, and what had happened afterward to me, and she lay perfectly still, listening, in a big chair.

304She was dressed to play golf, and I remember thinking she looked like a good illustration, her chin raised a little jauntily, her hair the colour of an autumn leaf, her face the same brown tint as the fingerless glove on her knee. 305When I had finished she told me without comment that she was engaged to another man. 306I doubted that, though there were several she could have married at a nod of her head, but I pretended to be surprised. 307For just a minute I wondered if I wasn’t making a mistake, then I thought it all over again quickly and got up to say goodbye.

308“Nevertheless you did throw me over,” said Jordan suddenly. 309“You threw me over on the telephone. 310I don’t give a damn about you now, but it was a new experience for me, and I felt a little dizzy for a while.”

311We shook hands.

312“Oh, and do you remember”—she added—“a conversation we had once about driving a car?”

313“Why—not exactly.”

314“You said a bad driver was only safe until she met another bad driver? 315Well, I met another bad driver, didn’t I? 316I mean it was careless of me to make such a wrong guess. 317I thought you were rather an honest, straightforward person. 318I thought it was your secret pride.”

319“I’m thirty,” I said. 320“I’m five years too old to lie to myself and call it honour.”

321She didn’t answer. 322Angry, and half in love with her, and tremendously sorry, I turned away.

323One afternoon late in October I saw Tom Buchanan. 324He was walking ahead of me along Fifth Avenue in his alert, aggressive way, his hands out a little from his body as if to fight off interference, his head moving sharply here and there, adapting itself to his restless eyes. 325Just as I slowed up to avoid overtaking him he stopped and began frowning into the windows of a jewellery store. 326Suddenly he saw me and walked back, holding out his hand.

327“What’s the matter, Nick? 328Do you object to shaking hands with me?”

329“Yes. 330You know what I think of you.”

331“You’re crazy, Nick,” he said quickly. 332“Crazy as hell. 333I don’t know what’s the matter with you.”

334“Tom,” I inquired, “what did you say to Wilson that afternoon?”

335He stared at me without a word, and I knew I had guessed right about those missing hours. 336I started to turn away, but he took a step after me and grabbed my arm.

337“I told him the truth,” he said. 338“He came to the door while we were getting ready to leave, and when I sent down word that we weren’t in he tried to force his way upstairs. 339He was crazy enough to kill me if I hadn’t told him who owned the car. 340His hand was on a revolver in his pocket every minute he was in the house—” He broke off defiantly. 341“What if I did tell him? 342That fellow had it coming to him. 343He threw dust into your eyes just like he did in Daisy’s, but he was a tough one. 344He ran over Myrtle like you’d run over a dog and never even stopped his car.”

345There was nothing I could say, except the one unutterable fact that it wasn’t true.

346“And if you think I didn’t have my share of suffering—look here, when I went to give up that flat and saw that damn box of dog biscuits sitting there on the sideboard, I sat down and cried like a baby. 347By God it was awful—”

348I couldn’t forgive him or like him, but I saw that what he had done was, to him, entirely justified. 349It was all very careless and confused. 350They were careless people, Tom and Daisy—they smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money or their vast carelessness, or whatever it was that kept them together, and let other people clean up the mess they had made…

351I shook hands with him; it seemed silly not to, for I felt suddenly as though I were talking to a child. 352Then he went into the jewellery store to buy a pearl necklace—or perhaps only a pair of cuff buttons—rid of my provincial squeamishness forever.

353Gatsby’s house was still empty when I left—the grass on his lawn had grown as long as mine. 354One of the taxi drivers in the village never took a fare past the entrance gate without stopping for a minute and pointing inside; perhaps it was he who drove Daisy and Gatsby over to East Egg the night of the accident, and perhaps he had made a story about it all his own. 355I didn’t want to hear it and I avoided him when I got off the train.

356I spent my Saturday nights in New York because those gleaming, dazzling parties of his were with me so vividly that I could still hear the music and the laughter, faint and incessant, from his garden, and the cars going up and down his drive. 357One night I did hear a material car there, and saw its lights stop at his front steps. 358But I didn’t investigate. 359Probably it was some final guest who had been away at the ends of the earth and didn’t know that the party was over.

360On the last night, with my trunk packed and my car sold to the grocer, I went over and looked at that huge incoherent failure of a house once more. 361On the white steps an obscene word, scrawled by some boy with a piece of brick, stood out clearly in the moonlight, and I erased it, drawing my shoe raspingly along the stone. 362Then I wandered down to the beach and sprawled out on the sand.

363Most of the big shore places were closed now and there were hardly any lights except the shadowy, moving glow of a ferryboat across the Sound. 364And as the moon rose higher the inessential houses began to melt away until gradually I became aware of the old island here that flowered once for Dutch sailors’ eyes—a fresh, green breast of the new world. 365Its vanished trees, the trees that had made way for Gatsby’s house, had once pandered in whispers to the last and greatest of all human dreams; for a transitory enchanted moment man must have held his breath in the presence of this continent, compelled into an aesthetic contemplation he neither understood nor desired, face to face for the last time in history with something commensurate to his capacity for wonder.

366And as I sat there brooding on the old, unknown world, I thought of Gatsby’s wonder when he first picked out the green light at the end of Daisy’s dock. 367He had come a long way to this blue lawn, and his dream must have seemed so close that he could hardly fail to grasp it. 368He did not know that it was already behind him, somewhere back in that vast obscurity beyond the city, where the dark fields of the republic rolled on under the night.

369Gatsby believed in the green light, the orgastic future that year by year recedes before us. 370It eluded us then, but that’s no matter—tomorrow we will run faster, stretch out our arms further… And one fine morning—

371So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past.
