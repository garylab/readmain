2When I came home to West Egg that night I was afraid for a moment that my house was on fire. 3Two o’clock and the whole corner of the peninsula was blazing with light, which fell unreal on the shrubbery and made thin elongating glints upon the roadside wires. 4Turning a corner, I saw that it was Gatsby’s house, lit from tower to cellar.

5At first I thought it was another party, a wild rout that had resolved itself into “hide-and-go-seek” or “sardines-in-the-box” with all the house thrown open to the game. 6But there wasn’t a sound. 7Only wind in the trees, which blew the wires and made the lights go off and on again as if the house had winked into the darkness. 8As my taxi groaned away I saw Gatsby walking toward me across his lawn.

9“Your place looks like the World’s Fair,” I said.

10“Does it?” 11He turned his eyes toward it absently. 12“I have been glancing into some of the rooms. 13Let’s go to Coney Island, old sport. 14In my car.”

15“It’s too late.”

16“Well, suppose we take a plunge in the swimming pool? 17I haven’t made use of it all summer.”

18“I’ve got to go to bed.”

19“All right.”

20He waited, looking at me with suppressed eagerness.

21“I talked with Miss Baker,” I said after a moment. 22“I’m going to call up Daisy tomorrow and invite her over here to tea.”

23“Oh, that’s all right,” he said carelessly. 24“I don’t want to put you to any trouble.”

25“What day would suit you?”

26“What day would suit you?” 27he corrected me quickly. 28“I don’t want to put you to any trouble, you see.”

29“How about the day after tomorrow?”

30He considered for a moment. 31Then, with reluctance: “I want to get the grass cut,” he said.

32We both looked down at the grass—there was a sharp line where my ragged lawn ended and the darker, well-kept expanse of his began. 33I suspected that he meant my grass.

34“There’s another little thing,” he said uncertainly, and hesitated.

35“Would you rather put it off for a few days?” 36I asked.

37“Oh, it isn’t about that. 38At least—” He fumbled with a series of beginnings. 39“Why, I thought—why, look here, old sport, you don’t make much money, do you?”

40“Not very much.”

41This seemed to reassure him and he continued more confidently.

42“I thought you didn’t, if you’ll pardon my—you see, I carry on a little business on the side, a sort of side line, you understand. 43And I thought that if you don’t make very much—You’re selling bonds, aren’t you, old sport?”

44“Trying to.”

45“Well, this would interest you. 46It wouldn’t take up much of your time and you might pick up a nice bit of money. 47It happens to be a rather confidential sort of thing.”

48I realize now that under different circumstances that conversation might have been one of the crises of my life. 49But, because the offer was obviously and tactlessly for a service to be rendered, I had no choice except to cut him off there.

50“I’ve got my hands full,” I said. 51“I’m much obliged but I couldn’t take on any more work.”

52“You wouldn’t have to do any business with Wolfshiem.” 53Evidently he thought that I was shying away from the “gonnegtion” mentioned at lunch, but I assured him he was wrong. 54He waited a moment longer, hoping I’d begin a conversation, but I was too absorbed to be responsive, so he went unwillingly home.

55The evening had made me lightheaded and happy; I think I walked into a deep sleep as I entered my front door. 56So I don’t know whether or not Gatsby went to Coney Island, or for how many hours he “glanced into rooms” while his house blazed gaudily on. 57I called up Daisy from the office next morning, and invited her to come to tea.

58“Don’t bring Tom,” I warned her.

59“What?”

60“Don’t bring Tom.”

61“Who is ‘Tom’?” 62she asked innocently.

63The day agreed upon was pouring rain. 64At eleven o’clock a man in a raincoat, dragging a lawn-mower, tapped at my front door and said that Mr. Gatsby had sent him over to cut my grass. 65This reminded me that I had forgotten to tell my Finn to come back, so I drove into West Egg Village to search for her among soggy whitewashed alleys and to buy some cups and lemons and flowers.

66The flowers were unnecessary, for at two o’clock a greenhouse arrived from Gatsby’s, with innumerable receptacles to contain it. 67An hour later the front door opened nervously, and Gatsby in a white flannel suit, silver shirt, and gold-coloured tie, hurried in. 68He was pale, and there were dark signs of sleeplessness beneath his eyes.

69“Is everything all right?” 70he asked immediately.

71“The grass looks fine, if that’s what you mean.”

72“What grass?” he inquired blankly. 73“Oh, the grass in the yard.” 74He looked out the window at it, but, judging from his expression, I don’t believe he saw a thing.

75“Looks very good,” he remarked vaguely. 76“One of the papers said they thought the rain would stop about four. 77I think it was The Journal. 78Have you got everything you need in the shape of—of tea?”

79I took him into the pantry, where he looked a little reproachfully at the Finn. 80Together we scrutinized the twelve lemon cakes from the delicatessen shop.

81“Will they do?” 82I asked.

83“Of course, of course! 84They’re fine!” 85and he added hollowly, “… old sport.”

86The rain cooled about half-past three to a damp mist, through which occasional thin drops swam like dew. 87Gatsby looked with vacant eyes through a copy of Clay’s Economics, starting at the Finnish tread that shook the kitchen floor, and peering towards the bleared windows from time to time as if a series of invisible but alarming happenings were taking place outside. 88Finally he got up and informed me, in an uncertain voice, that he was going home.

89“Why’s that?”

90“Nobody’s coming to tea. 91It’s too late!” 92He looked at his watch as if there was some pressing demand on his time elsewhere. 93“I can’t wait all day.”

94“Don’t be silly; it’s just two minutes to four.”

95He sat down miserably, as if I had pushed him, and simultaneously there was the sound of a motor turning into my lane. 96We both jumped up, and, a little harrowed myself, I went out into the yard.

97Under the dripping bare lilac-trees a large open car was coming up the drive. 98It stopped. 99Daisy’s face, tipped sideways beneath a three-cornered lavender hat, looked out at me with a bright ecstatic smile.

100“Is this absolutely where you live, my dearest one?”

101The exhilarating ripple of her voice was a wild tonic in the rain. 102I had to follow the sound of it for a moment, up and down, with my ear alone, before any words came through. 103A damp streak of hair lay like a dash of blue paint across her cheek, and her hand was wet with glistening drops as I took it to help her from the car.

104“Are you in love with me,” she said low in my ear, “or why did I have to come alone?”

105“That’s the secret of Castle Rackrent. 106Tell your chauffeur to go far away and spend an hour.”

107“Come back in an hour, Ferdie.” 108Then in a grave murmur: “His name is Ferdie.”

109“Does the gasoline affect his nose?”

110“I don’t think so,” she said innocently. 111“Why?”

112We went in. 113To my overwhelming surprise the living-room was deserted.

114“Well, that’s funny,” I exclaimed.

115“What’s funny?”

116She turned her head as there was a light dignified knocking at the front door. 117I went out and opened it. 118Gatsby, pale as death, with his hands plunged like weights in his coat pockets, was standing in a puddle of water glaring tragically into my eyes.

119With his hands still in his coat pockets he stalked by me into the hall, turned sharply as if he were on a wire, and disappeared into the living-room. 120It wasn’t a bit funny. 121Aware of the loud beating of my own heart I pulled the door to against the increasing rain.

122For half a minute there wasn’t a sound. 123Then from the living-room I heard a sort of choking murmur and part of a laugh, followed by Daisy’s voice on a clear artificial note:

124“I certainly am awfully glad to see you again.”

125A pause; it endured horribly. 126I had nothing to do in the hall, so I went into the room.

127Gatsby, his hands still in his pockets, was reclining against the mantelpiece in a strained counterfeit of perfect ease, even of boredom. 128His head leaned back so far that it rested against the face of a defunct mantelpiece clock, and from this position his distraught eyes stared down at Daisy, who was sitting, frightened but graceful, on the edge of a stiff chair.

129“We’ve met before,” muttered Gatsby. 130His eyes glanced momentarily at me, and his lips parted with an abortive attempt at a laugh. 131Luckily the clock took this moment to tilt dangerously at the pressure of his head, whereupon he turned and caught it with trembling fingers, and set it back in place. 132Then he sat down, rigidly, his elbow on the arm of the sofa and his chin in his hand.

133“I’m sorry about the clock,” he said.

134My own face had now assumed a deep tropical burn. 135I couldn’t muster up a single commonplace out of the thousand in my head.

136“It’s an old clock,” I told them idiotically.

137I think we all believed for a moment that it had smashed in pieces on the floor.

138“We haven’t met for many years,” said Daisy, her voice as matter-of-fact as it could ever be.

139“Five years next November.”

140The automatic quality of Gatsby’s answer set us all back at least another minute. 141I had them both on their feet with the desperate suggestion that they help me make tea in the kitchen when the demoniac Finn brought it in on a tray.

142Amid the welcome confusion of cups and cakes a certain physical decency established itself. 143Gatsby got himself into a shadow and, while Daisy and I talked, looked conscientiously from one to the other of us with tense, unhappy eyes. 144However, as calmness wasn’t an end in itself, I made an excuse at the first possible moment, and got to my feet.

145“Where are you going?” demanded Gatsby in immediate alarm.

146“I’ll be back.”

147“I’ve got to speak to you about something before you go.”

148He followed me wildly into the kitchen, closed the door, and whispered: “Oh, God!” 149in a miserable way.

150“What’s the matter?”

151“This is a terrible mistake,” he said, shaking his head from side to side, “a terrible, terrible mistake.”

152“You’re just embarrassed, that’s all,” and luckily I added: “Daisy’s embarrassed too.”

153“She’s embarrassed?” he repeated incredulously.

154“Just as much as you are.”

155“Don’t talk so loud.”

156“You’re acting like a little boy,” I broke out impatiently. 157“Not only that, but you’re rude. 158Daisy’s sitting in there all alone.”

159He raised his hand to stop my words, looked at me with unforgettable reproach, and, opening the door cautiously, went back into the other room.

160I walked out the back way—just as Gatsby had when he had made his nervous circuit of the house half an hour before—and ran for a huge black knotted tree, whose massed leaves made a fabric against the rain. 161Once more it was pouring, and my irregular lawn, well-shaved by Gatsby’s gardener, abounded in small muddy swamps and prehistoric marshes. 162There was nothing to look at from under the tree except Gatsby’s enormous house, so I stared at it, like Kant at his church steeple, for half an hour. 163A brewer had built it early in the “period” craze, a decade before, and there was a story that he’d agreed to pay five years’ taxes on all the neighbouring cottages if the owners would have their roofs thatched with straw. 164Perhaps their refusal took the heart out of his plan to Found a Family—he went into an immediate decline. 165His children sold his house with the black wreath still on the door. 166Americans, while willing, even eager, to be serfs, have always been obstinate about being peasantry.

167After half an hour, the sun shone again, and the grocer’s automobile rounded Gatsby’s drive with the raw material for his servants’ dinner—I felt sure he wouldn’t eat a spoonful. 168A maid began opening the upper windows of his house, appeared momentarily in each, and, leaning from the large central bay, spat meditatively into the garden. 169It was time I went back. 170While the rain continued it had seemed like the murmur of their voices, rising and swelling a little now and then with gusts of emotion. 171But in the new silence I felt that silence had fallen within the house too.

172I went in—after making every possible noise in the kitchen, short of pushing over the stove—but I don’t believe they heard a sound. 173They were sitting at either end of the couch, looking at each other as if some question had been asked, or was in the air, and every vestige of embarrassment was gone. 174Daisy’s face was smeared with tears, and when I came in she jumped up and began wiping at it with her handkerchief before a mirror. 175But there was a change in Gatsby that was simply confounding. 176He literally glowed; without a word or a gesture of exultation a new well-being radiated from him and filled the little room.

177“Oh, hello, old sport,” he said, as if he hadn’t seen me for years. 178I thought for a moment he was going to shake hands.

179“It’s stopped raining.”

180“Has it?” 181When he realized what I was talking about, that there were twinkle-bells of sunshine in the room, he smiled like a weather man, like an ecstatic patron of recurrent light, and repeated the news to Daisy. 182“What do you think of that? 183It’s stopped raining.”

184“I’m glad, Jay.” 185Her throat, full of aching, grieving beauty, told only of her unexpected joy.

186“I want you and Daisy to come over to my house,” he said, “I’d like to show her around.”

187“You’re sure you want me to come?”

188“Absolutely, old sport.”

189Daisy went upstairs to wash her face—too late I thought with humiliation of my towels—while Gatsby and I waited on the lawn.

190“My house looks well, doesn’t it?” 191he demanded. 192“See how the whole front of it catches the light.”

193I agreed that it was splendid.

194“Yes.” 195His eyes went over it, every arched door and square tower. 196“It took me just three years to earn the money that bought it.”

197“I thought you inherited your money.”

198“I did, old sport,” he said automatically, “but I lost most of it in the big panic—the panic of the war.”

199I think he hardly knew what he was saying, for when I asked him what business he was in he answered: “That’s my affair,” before he realized that it wasn’t an appropriate reply.

200“Oh, I’ve been in several things,” he corrected himself. 201“I was in the drug business and then I was in the oil business. 202But I’m not in either one now.” 203He looked at me with more attention. 204“Do you mean you’ve been thinking over what I proposed the other night?”

205Before I could answer, Daisy came out of the house and two rows of brass buttons on her dress gleamed in the sunlight.

206“That huge place there?” 207she cried pointing.

208“Do you like it?”

209“I love it, but I don’t see how you live there all alone.”

210“I keep it always full of interesting people, night and day. 211People who do interesting things. 212Celebrated people.”

213Instead of taking the shortcut along the Sound we went down to the road and entered by the big postern. 214With enchanting murmurs Daisy admired this aspect or that of the feudal silhouette against the sky, admired the gardens, the sparkling odour of jonquils and the frothy odour of hawthorn and plum blossoms and the pale gold odour of kiss-me-at-the-gate. 215It was strange to reach the marble steps and find no stir of bright dresses in and out the door, and hear no sound but bird voices in the trees.

216And inside, as we wandered through Marie Antoinette music-rooms and Restoration Salons, I felt that there were guests concealed behind every couch and table, under orders to be breathlessly silent until we had passed through. 217As Gatsby closed the door of “the Merton College Library” I could have sworn I heard the owl-eyed man break into ghostly laughter.

218We went upstairs, through period bedrooms swathed in rose and lavender silk and vivid with new flowers, through dressing-rooms and poolrooms, and bathrooms with sunken baths—intruding into one chamber where a dishevelled man in pyjamas was doing liver exercises on the floor. 219It was Mr. Klipspringer, the “boarder.” 220I had seen him wandering hungrily about the beach that morning. 221Finally we came to Gatsby’s own apartment, a bedroom and a bath, and an Adam’s study, where we sat down and drank a glass of some Chartreuse he took from a cupboard in the wall.

222He hadn’t once ceased looking at Daisy, and I think he revalued everything in his house according to the measure of response it drew from her well-loved eyes. 223Sometimes too, he stared around at his possessions in a dazed way, as though in her actual and astounding presence none of it was any longer real. 224Once he nearly toppled down a flight of stairs.

225His bedroom was the simplest room of all—except where the dresser was garnished with a toilet set of pure dull gold. 226Daisy took the brush with delight, and smoothed her hair, whereupon Gatsby sat down and shaded his eyes and began to laugh.

227“It’s the funniest thing, old sport,” he said hilariously. 228“I can’t—When I try to—”

229He had passed visibly through two states and was entering upon a third. 230After his embarrassment and his unreasoning joy he was consumed with wonder at her presence. 231He had been full of the idea so long, dreamed it right through to the end, waited with his teeth set, so to speak, at an inconceivable pitch of intensity. 232Now, in the reaction, he was running down like an over-wound clock.

233Recovering himself in a minute he opened for us two hulking patent cabinets which held his massed suits and dressing-gowns and ties, and his shirts, piled like bricks in stacks a dozen high.

234“I’ve got a man in England who buys me clothes. 235He sends over a selection of things at the beginning of each season, spring and fall.”

236He took out a pile of shirts and began throwing them, one by one, before us, shirts of sheer linen and thick silk and fine flannel, which lost their folds as they fell and covered the table in many-coloured disarray. 237While we admired he brought more and the soft rich heap mounted higher—shirts with stripes and scrolls and plaids in coral and apple-green and lavender and faint orange, with monograms of indian blue. 238Suddenly, with a strained sound, Daisy bent her head into the shirts and began to cry stormily.

239“They’re such beautiful shirts,” she sobbed, her voice muffled in the thick folds. 240“It makes me sad because I’ve never seen such—such beautiful shirts before.”

241After the house, we were to see the grounds and the swimming pool, and the hydroplane, and the midsummer flowers—but outside Gatsby’s window it began to rain again, so we stood in a row looking at the corrugated surface of the Sound.

242“If it wasn’t for the mist we could see your home across the bay,” said Gatsby. 243“You always have a green light that burns all night at the end of your dock.”

244Daisy put her arm through his abruptly, but he seemed absorbed in what he had just said. 245Possibly it had occurred to him that the colossal significance of that light had now vanished forever. 246Compared to the great distance that had separated him from Daisy it had seemed very near to her, almost touching her. 247It had seemed as close as a star to the moon. 248Now it was again a green light on a dock. 249His count of enchanted objects had diminished by one.

250I began to walk about the room, examining various indefinite objects in the half darkness. 251A large photograph of an elderly man in yachting costume attracted me, hung on the wall over his desk.

252“Who’s this?”

253“That? 254That’s Mr. Dan Cody, old sport.”

255The name sounded faintly familiar.

256“He’s dead now. 257He used to be my best friend years ago.”

258There was a small picture of Gatsby, also in yachting costume, on the bureau—Gatsby with his head thrown back defiantly—taken apparently when he was about eighteen.

259“I adore it,” exclaimed Daisy. 260“The pompadour! 261You never told me you had a pompadour—or a yacht.”

262“Look at this,” said Gatsby quickly. 263“Here’s a lot of clippings—about you.”

264They stood side by side examining it. 265I was going to ask to see the rubies when the phone rang, and Gatsby took up the receiver.

266“Yes… Well, I can’t talk now… I can’t talk now, old sport… I said a small town… 267He must know what a small town is… Well, he’s no use to us if Detroit is his idea of a small town…”

268He rang off.

269“Come here quick!” 270cried Daisy at the window.

271The rain was still falling, but the darkness had parted in the west, and there was a pink and golden billow of foamy clouds above the sea.

272“Look at that,” she whispered, and then after a moment: “I’d like to just get one of those pink clouds and put you in it and push you around.”

273I tried to go then, but they wouldn’t hear of it; perhaps my presence made them feel more satisfactorily alone.

274“I know what we’ll do,” said Gatsby, “we’ll have Klipspringer play the piano.”

275He went out of the room calling “Ewing!” and returned in a few minutes accompanied by an embarrassed, slightly worn young man, with shell-rimmed glasses and scanty blond hair. 276He was now decently clothed in a “sport shirt,” open at the neck, sneakers, and duck trousers of a nebulous hue.

277“Did we interrupt your exercise?” inquired Daisy politely.

278“I was asleep,” cried Mr. Klipspringer, in a spasm of embarrassment. 279“That is, I’d been asleep. 280Then I got up…”

281“Klipspringer plays the piano,” said Gatsby, cutting him off. 282“Don’t you, Ewing, old sport?”

283“I don’t play well. 284I don’t—hardly play at all. 285I’m all out of prac—”

286“We’ll go downstairs,” interrupted Gatsby. 287He flipped a switch. 288The grey windows disappeared as the house glowed full of light.

289In the music-room Gatsby turned on a solitary lamp beside the piano. 290He lit Daisy’s cigarette from a trembling match, and sat down with her on a couch far across the room, where there was no light save what the gleaming floor bounced in from the hall.

291When Klipspringer had played “The Love Nest” he turned around on the bench and searched unhappily for Gatsby in the gloom.

292“I’m all out of practice, you see. 293I told you I couldn’t play. 294I’m all out of prac—”

295“Don’t talk so much, old sport,” commanded Gatsby. 296“Play!”

297“In the morning, In the evening, Ain’t we got fun—”

301Outside the wind was loud and there was a faint flow of thunder along the Sound. 302All the lights were going on in West Egg now; the electric trains, men-carrying, were plunging home through the rain from New York. 303It was the hour of a profound human change, and excitement was generating on the air.

304“One thing’s sure and nothing’s surer The rich get richer and the poor get—children. 305In the meantime, In between time—”

310As I went over to say goodbye I saw that the expression of bewilderment had come back into Gatsby’s face, as though a faint doubt had occurred to him as to the quality of his present happiness. 311Almost five years! 312There must have been moments even that afternoon when Daisy tumbled short of his dreams—not through her own fault, but because of the colossal vitality of his illusion. 313It had gone beyond her, beyond everything. 314He had thrown himself into it with a creative passion, adding to it all the time, decking it out with every bright feather that drifted his way. 315No amount of fire or freshness can challenge what a man can store up in his ghostly heart.

316As I watched him he adjusted himself a little, visibly. 317His hand took hold of hers, and as she said something low in his ear he turned toward her with a rush of emotion. 318I think that voice held him most, with its fluctuating, feverish warmth, because it couldn’t be over-dreamed—that voice was a deathless song.

319They had forgotten me, but Daisy glanced up and held out her hand; Gatsby didn’t know me now at all. 320I looked once more at them and they looked back at me, remotely, possessed by intense life. 321Then I went out of the room and down the marble steps into the rain, leaving them there together.
