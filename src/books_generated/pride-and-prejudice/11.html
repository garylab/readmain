1CHAPTER XI.

2WHEN the ladies removed after dinner Elizabeth ran up to her sister, and seeing her well guarded from cold, attended her into the drawing-room, where she was welcomed by her two friends with many professions of pleasure; and Elizabeth had never seen them so agreeable as they were during the hour which passed before the gentlemen appeared. 3Their powers of conversation were considerable. 4They could describe an entertainment with accuracy, relate an anecdote with humour, and laugh at their acquaintance with spirit.

5But when the gentlemen entered, Jane was no longer the first object; Miss Bingley’s eyes were instantly turned towards Darcy, and she had something to say to him before he had advanced many steps. 6He addressed himself directly to Miss Bennet with a polite congratulation; Mr. Hurst also made her a slight bow, and said he was “very glad;” but diffuseness and warmth remained for Bingley’s salutation. 7He was full of joy and attention. 8The first half hour was spent in piling up the fire, lest she should suffer from the change of room; and she removed, at his desire, to the other side of the fireplace, that she might be farther from the door. 9He then sat down by her, and talked scarcely to anyone else. 10Elizabeth, at work in the opposite corner, saw it all with great delight.

11When tea was over Mr. Hurst reminded his sister-in-law of the card-table—but in vain. 12She had obtained private intelligence that Mr. Darcy did not wish for cards, and Mr. Hurst soon found even his open petition rejected. 13She assured him that no one intended to play, and the silence of the whole party on the subject seemed to justify her. 14Mr. Hurst had, therefore, nothing to do but to stretch himself on one of the sofas and go to sleep. 15Darcy took up a book. 16Miss Bingley did the same; and Mrs. Hurst, principally occupied in playing with her bracelets and rings, joined now and then in her brother’s conversation with Miss Bennet.

17Miss Bingley’s attention was quite as much engaged in watching Mr. Darcy’s progress through his book, as in reading her own; and she was perpetually either making some inquiry, or looking at his page. 18She could not win him, however, to any conversation; he merely answered her question and read on. 19At length, quite exhausted by the attempt to be amused with her own book, which she had only chosen because it was the second volume of his, she gave a great yawn and said, “How pleasant it is to spend an evening in this way! 20I declare, after all, there is no enjoyment like reading! 21How much sooner one tires of anything than of a book! 22When I have a house of my own, I shall be miserable if I have not an excellent library.”

23No one made any reply. 24She then yawned again, threw aside her book, and cast her eyes round the room in quest of some amusement; when, hearing her brother mentioning a ball to Miss Bennet, she turned suddenly towards him and said,—

25“By the bye Charles, are you really serious in meditating a dance at Netherfield? 26I would advise you, before you determine on it, to consult the wishes of the present party; I am much mistaken if there are not some among us to whom a ball would be rather a punishment than a pleasure.”

27“If you mean Darcy,” cried her brother, “he may go to bed, if he chooses, before it begins; but as for the ball, it is quite a settled thing, and as soon as Nicholls has made white soup enough I shall send round my cards.”

28“I should like balls infinitely better,” she replied, “if they were carried on in a different manner; but there is something insufferably tedious in the usual process of such a meeting. 29It would surely be much more rational if conversation instead of dancing made the order of the day.”

30“Much more rational, my dear Caroline, I dare say; but it would not be near so much like a ball.”

31Miss Bingley made no answer, and soon afterwards got up and walked about the room. 32Her figure was elegant, and she walked well; but Darcy, at whom it was all aimed, was still inflexibly studious. 33In the desperation of her feelings, she resolved on one effort more; and, turning to Elizabeth, said,—

34“Miss Eliza Bennet, let me persuade you to follow my example, and take a turn about the room. 35I assure you it is very refreshing after sitting so long in one attitude.”

36Elizabeth was surprised, but agreed to it immediately. 37Miss Bingley succeeded no less in the real object of her civility: Mr. Darcy looked up. 38He was as much awake to the novelty of attention in that quarter as Elizabeth herself could be, and unconsciously closed his book. 39He was directly invited to join their party, but he declined it, observing that he could imagine but two motives for their choosing to walk up and down the room together, with either of which motives his joining them would interfere. 40What could he mean? 41She was dying to know what could be his meaning—and asked Elizabeth whether she could at all understand him.

42“Not at all,” was her answer; “but, depend upon it, he means to be severe on us, and our surest way of disappointing him will be to ask nothing about it.”

43Miss Bingley, however, was incapable of disappointing Mr. Darcy in anything, and persevered, therefore, in requiring an explanation of his two motives.

44“I have not the smallest objection to explaining them,” said he, as soon as she allowed him to speak. 45“You either choose this method of passing the evening because you are in each other’s confidence, and have secret affairs to discuss, or because you are conscious that your figures appear to the greatest advantage in walking: if the first, I should be completely in your way; and if the second, I can admire you much better as I sit by the fire.”

46“Oh, shocking!” cried Miss Bingley. 47“I never heard anything so abominable. 48How shall we punish him for such a speech?”

49“Nothing so easy, if you have but the inclination,” said Elizabeth. 50“We can all plague and punish one another. 51Tease him—laugh at him. 52Intimate as you are, you must know how it is to be done.”

53“But upon my honour I do not. 54I do assure you that my intimacy has not yet taught me that. 55Tease calmness of temper and presence of mind! 56No, no; I feel he may defy us there. 57And as to laughter, we will not expose ourselves, if you please, by attempting to laugh without a subject. 58Mr. Darcy may hug himself.”

59“Mr. Darcy is not to be laughed at!” cried Elizabeth. 60“That is an uncommon advantage, and uncommon I hope it will continue, for it would be a great loss to me to have many such acquaintance. 61I dearly love a laugh.”

62“Miss Bingley,” said he, “has given me credit for more than can be. 63The wisest and best of men,—nay, the wisest and best of their actions,—may be rendered ridiculous by a person whose first object in life is a joke.”

64“Certainly,” replied Elizabeth, “there are such people, but I hope I am not one of them. 65I hope I never ridicule what is wise or good. 66Follies and nonsense, whims and inconsistencies, do divert me, I own, and I laugh at them whenever I can. 67But these, I suppose, are precisely what you are without.”

68“Perhaps that is not possible for anyone. 69But it has been the study of my life to avoid those weaknesses which often expose a strong understanding to ridicule.”

70“Such as vanity and pride.”

71“Yes, vanity is a weakness indeed. 72But pride—where there is a real superiority of mind—pride will be always under good regulation.”

73Elizabeth turned away to hide a smile.

74“Your examination of Mr. Darcy is over, I presume,” said Miss Bingley; “and pray what is the result?”

75“I am perfectly convinced by it that Mr. Darcy has no defect. 76He owns it himself without disguise.”

77“No,” said Darcy, “I have made no such pretension. 78I have faults enough, but they are not, I hope, of understanding. 79My temper I dare not vouch for. 80It is, I believe, too little yielding; certainly too little for the convenience of the world. 81I cannot forget the follies and vices of others so soon as I ought, nor their offences against myself. 82My feelings are not puffed about with every attempt to move them. 83My temper would perhaps be called resentful. 84My good opinion once lost is lost for ever.”

85“That is a failing, indeed!” cried Elizabeth. 86“Implacable resentment is a shade in a character. 87But you have chosen your fault well. 88I really cannot laugh at it. 89You are safe from me.”

90“There is, I believe, in every disposition a tendency to some particular evil, a natural defect, which not even the best education can overcome.”

91“And your defect is a propensity to hate everybody.”

92“And yours,” he replied, with a smile, “is wilfully to misunderstand them.”

93“Do let us have a little music,” cried Miss Bingley, tired of a conversation in which she had no share. 94“Louisa, you will not mind my waking Mr. Hurst.”

95Her sister made not the smallest objection, and the pianoforte was opened; and Darcy, after a few moments’ recollection, was not sorry for it. 96He began to feel the danger of paying Elizabeth too much attention.
