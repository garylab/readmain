1CHAPTER XXXIX.

2IT was the second week in May, in which the three young ladies set out together from Gracechurch Street for the town of ——, in Hertfordshire; and, as they drew near the appointed inn where Mr. Bennet’s carriage was to meet them, they quickly perceived, in token of the coachman’s punctuality, both Kitty and Lydia looking out of a dining-room upstairs. 3These two girls had been above an hour in the place, happily employed in visiting an opposite milliner, watching the sentinel on guard, and dressing a salad and cucumber.

4After welcoming their sisters, they triumphantly displayed a table set out with such cold meat as an inn larder usually affords, exclaiming, “Is not this nice? 5is not this an agreeable surprise?”

6“And we mean to treat you all,” added Lydia; “but you must lend us the money, for we have just spent ours at the shop out there.” 7Then showing her purchases,—“Look here, I have bought this bonnet. 8I do not think it is very pretty; but I thought I might as well buy it as not. 9I shall pull it to pieces as soon as I get home, and see if I can make it up any better.”

10And when her sisters abused it as ugly, she added, with perfect unconcern, “Oh, but there were two or three much uglier in the shop; and when I have bought some prettier-coloured satin to trim it with fresh, I think it will be very tolerable. 11Besides, it will not much signify what one wears this summer, after the ——shire have left Meryton, and they are going in a fortnight.”

12“Are they, indeed?” cried Elizabeth, with the greatest satisfaction.

13“They are going to be encamped near Brighton; and I do so want papa to take us all there for the summer! 14It would be such a delicious scheme, and I dare say would hardly cost anything at all. 15Mamma would like to go, too, of all things! 16Only think what a miserable summer else we shall have!”

17“Yes,” thought Elizabeth; “that would be a delightful scheme, indeed, and completely do for us at once. 18Good Heaven! 19Brighton and a whole campful of soldiers, to us, who have been overset already by one poor regiment of militia, and the monthly balls of Meryton!”

20“Now I have got some news for you,” said Lydia, as they sat down to table. 21“What do you think? 22It is excellent news, capital news, and about a certain person that we all like.”

23Jane and Elizabeth looked at each other, and the waiter was told that he need not stay. 24Lydia laughed, and said,—

25“Ay, that is just like your formality and discretion. 26You thought the waiter must not hear, as if he cared! 27I dare say he often hears worse things said than I am going to say. 28But he is an ugly fellow! 29I am glad he is gone. 30I never saw such a long chin in my life. 31Well, but now for my news: it is about dear Wickham; too good for the waiter, is not it? 32There is no danger of Wickham’s marrying Mary King—there’s for you! 33She is gone down to her uncle at Liverpool; gone to stay. 34Wickham is safe.”

35“And Mary King is safe!” 36added Elizabeth; “safe from a connection imprudent as to fortune.”

37“She is a great fool for going away, if she liked him.”

38“But I hope there is no strong attachment on either side,” said Jane.

39“I am sure there is not on his. 40I will answer for it, he never cared three straws about her. 41Who could about such a nasty little freckled thing?”

42Elizabeth was shocked to think that, however incapable of such coarseness of expression herself, the coarseness of the sentiment was little other than her own breast had formerly harboured and fancied liberal!

43As soon as all had ate, and the elder ones paid, the carriage was ordered; and, after some contrivance, the whole party, with all their boxes, workbags, and parcels, and the unwelcome addition of Kitty’s and Lydia’s purchases, were seated in it.

44“How nicely we are crammed in!” cried Lydia. 45“I am glad I brought my bonnet, if it is only for the fun of having another band-box! 46Well, now let us be quite comfortable and snug, and talk and laugh all the way home. 47And in the first place, let us hear what has happened to you all since you went away. 48Have you seen any pleasant men? 49Have you had any flirting? 50I was in great hopes that one of you would have got a husband before you came back. 51Jane will be quite an old maid soon, I declare. 52She is almost three-and-twenty! 53Lord! 54how ashamed I should be of not being married before three-and-twenty! 55My aunt Philips wants you so to get husbands you can’t think. 56She says Lizzy had better have taken Mr. Collins; but I do not think there would have been any fun in it. 57Lord! 58how I should like to be married before any of you! 59and then I would chaperon you about to all the balls. 60Dear me! 61we had such a good piece of fun the other day at Colonel Forster’s! 62Kitty and me were to spend the day there, and Mrs. Forster promised to have a little dance in the evening; (by-the-bye, Mrs. Forster and me are such friends!) 63and so she asked the two Harringtons to come: but Harriet was ill, and so Pen was forced to come by herself; and then, what do you think we did? 64We dressed up Chamberlayne in woman’s clothes, on purpose to pass for a lady,—only think what fun! 65Not a soul knew of it, but Colonel and Mrs. Forster, and Kitty and me, except my aunt, for we were forced to borrow one of her gowns; and you cannot imagine how well he looked! 66When Denny, and Wickham, and Pratt, and two or three more of the men came in, they did not know him in the least. 67Lord! 68how I laughed! 69and so did Mrs. Forster. 70I thought I should have died. 71And that made the men suspect something, and then they soon found out what was the matter.”

72With such kind of histories of their parties and good jokes did Lydia, assisted by Kitty’s hints and additions, endeavour to amuse her companions all the way to Longbourn. 73Elizabeth listened as little as she could, but there was no escaping the frequent mention of Wickham’s name.

74Their reception at home was most kind. 75Mrs. Bennet rejoiced to see Jane in undiminished beauty; and more than once during dinner did Mr. Bennet say voluntarily to Elizabeth,——

76“I am glad you are come back, Lizzy.”

77Their party in the dining-room was large, for almost all the Lucases came to meet Maria and hear the news; and various were the subjects which occupied them: Lady Lucas was inquiring of Maria, across the table, after the welfare and poultry of her eldest daughter; Mrs. Bennet was doubly engaged, on one hand collecting an account of the present fashions from Jane, who sat some way below her, and on the other, retailing them all to the younger Miss Lucases; and Lydia, in a voice rather louder than any other person’s, was enumerating the various pleasures of the morning to anybody who would hear her.

78“Oh, Mary,” said she, “I wish you had gone with us, for we had such fun! as we went along Kitty and me drew up all the blinds, and pretended there was nobody in the coach; and I should have gone so all the way, if Kitty had not been sick; and when we got to the George, I do think we behaved very handsomely, for we treated the other three with the nicest cold luncheon in the world, and if you would have gone, we would have treated you too. 79And then when we came away it was such fun! 80I thought we never should have got into the coach. 81I was ready to die of laughter. 82And then we were so merry all the way home! 83we talked and laughed so loud, that anybody might have heard us ten miles off!”

84To this, Mary very gravely replied, “Far be it from me, my dear sister, to depreciate such pleasures. 85They would doubtless be congenial with the generality of female minds. 86But I confess they would have no charms for me. 87I should infinitely prefer a book.”

88But of this answer Lydia heard not a word. 89She seldom listened to anybody for more than half a minute, and never attended to Mary at all.

90In the afternoon Lydia was urgent with the rest of the girls to walk to Meryton, and see how everybody went on; but Elizabeth steadily opposed the scheme. 91It should not be said, that the Miss Bennets could not be at home half a day before they were in pursuit of the officers. 92There was another reason, too, for her opposition. 93She dreaded seeing Wickham again, and was resolved to avoid it as long as possible. 94The comfort to her, of the regiment’s approaching removal, was indeed beyond expression. 95In a fortnight they were to go, and once gone, she hoped there could be nothing more to plague her on his account.

96She had not been many hours at home, before she found that the Brighton scheme, of which Lydia had given them a hint at the inn, was under frequent discussion between her parents. 97Elizabeth saw directly that her father had not the smallest intention of yielding; but his answers were at the same time so vague and equivocal, that her mother, though often disheartened, had never yet despaired of succeeding at last.
