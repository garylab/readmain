1CHAPTER LII.

2ELIZABETH had the satisfaction of receiving an answer to her letter as soon as she possibly could. 3She was no sooner in possession of it, than hurrying into the little copse, where she was least likely to be interrupted, she sat down on one of the benches, and prepared to be happy; for the length of the letter convinced her that it did not contain a denial.

4“Gracechurch Street, Sept. 6.

5“My dear Niece,

6“I have just received your letter, and shall devote this whole morning to answering it, as I foresee that a little writing will not comprise what I have to tell you. 7I must confess myself surprised by your application; I did not expect it from you. 8Don’t think me angry, however, for I only mean to let you know, that I had not imagined such inquiries to be necessary on your side. 9If you do not choose to understand me, forgive my impertinence. 10Your uncle is as much surprised as I am; and nothing but the belief of your being a party concerned would have allowed him to act as he has done. 11But if you are really innocent and ignorant, I must be more explicit. 12On the very day of my coming home from Longbourn, your uncle had a most unexpected visitor. 13Mr. Darcy called, and was shut up with him several hours. 14It was all over before I arrived; so my curiosity was not so dreadfully racked as yours seems to have been. 15He came to tell Mr. Gardiner that he had found out where your sister and Mr. Wickham were, and that he had seen and talked with them both—Wickham repeatedly, Lydia once. 16From what I can collect, he left Derbyshire only one day after ourselves, and came to town with the resolution of hunting for them. 17The motive professed was his conviction of its being owing to himself that Wickham’s worthlessness had not been so well known as to make it impossible for any young woman of character to love or confide in him. 18He generously imputed the whole to his mistaken pride, and confessed that he had before thought it beneath him to lay his private actions open to the world. 19His character was to speak for itself. 20He called it, therefore, his duty to step forward, and endeavour to remedy an evil which had been brought on by himself. 21If he had another motive, I am sure it would never disgrace him. 22He had been some days in town before he was able to discover them; but he had something to direct his search, which was more than we had; and the consciousness of this was another reason for his resolving to follow us. 23There is a lady, it seems, a Mrs. Younge, who was some time ago governess to Miss Darcy, and was dismissed from her charge on some cause of disapprobation, though he did not say what. 24She then took a large house in Edward Street, and has since maintained herself by letting lodgings. 25This Mrs. Younge was, he knew, intimately acquainted with Wickham; and he went to her for intelligence of him, as soon as he got to town. 26But it was two or three days before he could get from her what he wanted. 27She would not betray her trust, I suppose, without bribery and corruption, for she really did know where her friend was to be found. 28Wickham, indeed, had gone to her on their first arrival in London; and had she been able to receive them into her house, they would have taken up their abode with her. 29At length, however, our kind friend procured the wished-for direction. 30They were in —— Street. 31He saw Wickham, and afterwards insisted on seeing Lydia. 32His first object with her, he acknowledged, had been to persuade her to quit her present disgraceful situation, and return to her friends as soon as they could be prevailed on to receive her, offering his assistance as far as it would go. 33But he found Lydia absolutely resolved on remaining where she was. 34She cared for none of her friends; she wanted no help of his; she would not hear of leaving Wickham. 35She was sure they should be married some time or other, and it did not much signify when. 36Since such were her feelings, it only remained, he thought, to secure and expedite a marriage, which, in his very first conversation with Wickham, he easily learnt had never been his design. 37He confessed himself obliged to leave the regiment on account of some debts of honour which were very pressing; and scrupled not to lay all the ill consequences of Lydia’s flight on her own folly alone. 38He meant to resign his commission immediately; and as to his future situation, he could conjecture very little about it. 39He must go somewhere, but he did not know where, and he knew he should have nothing to live on. 40Mr. Darcy asked why he did not marry your sister at once. 41Though Mr. Bennet was not imagined to be very rich, he would have been able to do something for him, and his situation must have been benefited by marriage. 42But he found, in reply to this question, that Wickham still cherished the hope of more effectually making his fortune by marriage, in some other country. 43Under such circumstances, however, he was not likely to be proof against the temptation of immediate relief. 44They met several times, for there was much to be discussed. 45Wickham, of course, wanted more than he could get; but at length was reduced to be reasonable. 46Everything being settled between them, Mr. Darcy’s next step was to make your uncle acquainted with it, and he first called in Gracechurch Street the evening before I came home. 47But Mr. Gardiner could not be seen; and Mr. Darcy found, on further inquiry, that your father was still with him, but would quit town the next morning. 48He did not judge your father to be a person whom he could so properly consult as your uncle, and therefore readily postponed seeing him till after the departure of the former. 49He did not leave his name, and till the next day it was only known that a gentleman had called on business. 50On Saturday he came again. 51Your father was gone, your uncle at home, and, as I said before, they had a great deal of talk together. 52They met again on Sunday, and then I saw him too. 53It was not all settled before Monday: as soon as it was, the express was sent off to Longbourn. 54But our visitor was very obstinate. 55I fancy, Lizzy, that obstinacy is the real defect of his character, after all. 56He has been accused of many faults at different times; but this is the true one. 57Nothing was to be done that he did not do himself; though I am sure (and I do not speak it to be thanked, therefore say nothing about it) your uncle would most readily have settled the whole. 58They battled it together for a long time, which was more than either the gentleman or lady concerned in it deserved. 59But at last your uncle was forced to yield, and instead of being allowed to be of use to his niece, was forced to put up with only having the probable credit of it, which went sorely against the grain; and I really believe your letter this morning gave him great pleasure, because it required an explanation that would rob him of his borrowed feathers, and give the praise where it was due. 60But, Lizzy, this must go no further than yourself, or Jane at most. 61You know pretty well, I suppose, what has been done for the young people. 62His debts are to be paid, amounting, I believe, to considerably more than a thousand pounds, another thousand in addition to her own settled upon her, and his commission purchased. 63The reason why all this was to be done by him alone, was such as I have given above. 64It was owing to him, to his reserve and want of proper consideration, that Wickham’s character had been so misunderstood, and consequently that he had been received and noticed as he was. 65Perhaps there was some truth in this; though I doubt whether his reserve, or anybody’s reserve can be answerable for the event. 66But in spite of all this fine talking, my dear Lizzy, you may rest perfectly assured that your uncle would never have yielded, if we had not given him credit for another interest in the affair. 67When all this was resolved on, he returned again to his friends, who were still staying at Pemberley; but it was agreed that he should be in London once more when the wedding took place, and all money matters were then to receive the last finish. 68I believe I have now told you everything. 69It is a relation which you tell me is to give you great surprise; I hope at least it will not afford you any displeasure. 70Lydia came to us, and Wickham had constant admission to the house. 71He was exactly what he had been when I knew him in Hertfordshire; but I would not tell you how little I was satisfied with her behaviour while she stayed with us, if I had not perceived, by Jane’s letter last Wednesday, that her conduct on coming home was exactly of a piece with it, and therefore what I now tell you can give you no fresh pain. 72I talked to her repeatedly in the most serious manner, representing to her the wickedness of what she had done, and all the unhappiness she had brought on her family. 73If she heard me, it was by good luck, for I am sure she did not listen. 74I was sometimes quite provoked; but then I recollected my dear Elizabeth and Jane, and for their sakes had patience with her. 75Mr. Darcy was punctual in his return, and, as Lydia informed you, attended the wedding. 76He dined with us the next day, and was to leave town again on Wednesday or Thursday. 77Will you be very angry with me, my dear Lizzy, if I take this opportunity of saying (what I was never bold enough to say before) how much I like him? 78His behaviour to us has, in every respect, been as pleasing as when we were in Derbyshire. 79His understanding and opinions all please me; he wants nothing but a little more liveliness, and that, if he marry prudently, his wife may teach him. 80I thought him very sly; he hardly ever mentioned your name. 81But slyness seems the fashion. 82Pray forgive me, if I have been very presuming, or at least do not punish me so far as to exclude me from P. 83I shall never be quite happy till I have been all round the park. 84A low phaeton with a nice little pair of ponies would be the very thing. 85But I must write no more. 86The children have been wanting me this half hour.

87“Yours, very sincerely, “M. Gardiner.”

89The contents of this letter threw Elizabeth into a flutter of spirits, in which it was difficult to determine whether pleasure or pain bore the greatest share. 90The vague and unsettled suspicions which uncertainty had produced, of what Mr. Darcy might have been doing to forward her sister’s match—which she had feared to encourage, as an exertion of goodness too great to be probable, and at the same time dreaded to be just, from the pain of obligation—were proved beyond their greatest extent to be true! 91He had followed them purposely to town, he had taken on himself all the trouble and mortification attendant on such a research; in which supplication had been necessary to a woman whom he must abominate and despise, and where he was reduced to meet, frequently meet, reason with, persuade, and finally bribe the man whom he always most wished to avoid, and whose very name it was punishment to him to pronounce. 92He had done all this for a girl whom he could neither regard nor esteem. 93Her heart did whisper that he had done it for her. 94But it was a hope shortly checked by other considerations; and she soon felt that even her vanity was insufficient, when required to depend on his affection for her, for a woman who had already refused him, as able to overcome a sentiment so natural as abhorrence against relationship with Wickham. 95Brother-in-law of Wickham! 96Every kind of pride must revolt from the connection. 97He had, to be sure, done much. 98She was ashamed to think how much. 99But he had given a reason for his interference, which asked no extraordinary stretch of belief. 100It was reasonable that he should feel he had been wrong; he had liberality, and he had the means of exercising it; and though she would not place herself as his principal inducement, she could perhaps believe, that remaining partiality for her might assist his endeavours in a cause where her peace of mind must be materially concerned. 101It was painful, exceedingly painful, to know that they were under obligations to a person who could never receive a return. 102They owed the restoration of Lydia, her character, everything to him. 103Oh, how heartily did she grieve over every ungracious sensation she had ever encouraged, every saucy speech she had ever directed towards him! 104For herself she was humbled; but she was proud of him,—proud that in a cause of compassion and honour he had been able to get the better of himself. 105She read over her aunt’s commendation of him again and again. 106It was hardly enough; but it pleased her. 107She was even sensible of some pleasure, though mixed with regret, on finding how steadfastly both she and her uncle had been persuaded that affection and confidence subsisted between Mr. Darcy and herself.

108She was roused from her seat and her reflections, by someone’s approach; and, before she could strike into another path, she was overtaken by Wickham.

109“I am afraid I interrupt your solitary ramble, my dear sister?” said he, as he joined her.

110“You certainly do,” she replied with a smile; “but it does not follow that the interruption must be unwelcome.”

111“I should be sorry, indeed, if it were. 112We were always good friends, and now we are better.”

113“True. 114Are the others coming out?”

115“I do not know. 116Mrs. Bennet and Lydia are going in the carriage to Meryton. 117And so, my dear sister, I find, from our uncle and aunt, that you have actually seen Pemberley.”

118She replied in the affirmative.

119“I almost envy you the pleasure, and yet I believe it would be too much for me, or else I could take it in my way to Newcastle. 120And you saw the old housekeeper, I suppose? 121Poor Reynolds, she was always very fond of me. 122But of course she did not mention my name to you.”

123“Yes, she did.”

124“And what did she say?”

125“That you were gone into the army, and she was afraid had—not turned out well. 126At such a distance as that, you know, things are strangely misrepresented.”

127“Certainly,” he replied, biting his lips. 128Elizabeth hoped she had silenced him; but he soon afterwards said,—

129“I was surprised to see Darcy in town last month. 130We passed each other several times. 131I wonder what he can be doing there.”

132“Perhaps preparing for his marriage with Miss de Bourgh,” said Elizabeth. 133“It must be something particular to take him there at this time of year.”

134“Undoubtedly. 135Did you see him while you were at Lambton? 136I thought I understood from the Gardiners that you had.”

137“Yes; he introduced us to his sister.”

138“And do you like her?”

139“Very much.”

140“I have heard, indeed, that she is uncommonly improved within this year or two. 141When I last saw her, she was not very promising. 142I am very glad you liked her. 143I hope she will turn out well.”

144“I dare say she will; she has got over the most trying age.”

145“Did you go by the village of Kympton?”

146“I do not recollect that we did.”

147“I mention it because it is the living which I ought to have had. 148A most delightful place! 149Excellent parsonage-house! 150It would have suited me in every respect.”

151“How should you have liked making sermons?”

152“Exceedingly well. 153I should have considered it as part of my duty, and the exertion would soon have been nothing. 154One ought not to repine; but, to be sure, it would have been such a thing for me! 155The quiet, the retirement of such a life, would have answered all my ideas of happiness! 156But it was not to be. 157Did you ever hear Darcy mention the circumstance when you were in Kent?”

158“I have heard from authority, which I thought as good, that it was left you conditionally only, and at the will of the present patron.”

159“You have! 160Yes, there was something in that; I told you so from the first, you may remember.”

161“I did hear, too, that there was a time when sermon-making was not so palatable to you as it seems to be at present; that you actually declared your resolution of never taking orders, and that the business had been compromised accordingly.”

162“You did! 163and it was not wholly without foundation. 164You may remember what I told you on that point, when first we talked of it.”

165They were now almost at the door of the house, for she had walked fast to get rid of him; and unwilling, for her sister’s sake, to provoke him, she only said in reply, with a good-humoured smile,—

166“Come, Mr. Wickham, we are brother and sister, you know. 167Do not let us quarrel about the past. 168In future, I hope we shall be always of one mind.”

169She held out her hand: he kissed it with affectionate gallantry, though he hardly knew how to look, and they entered the house.
