CHAPTER LV.

1A FEW days after this visit, Mr. Bingley called again, and alone. 2His friend had left him that morning for London, but was to return home in ten days’ time. 3He sat with them above an hour, and was in remarkably good spirits. 4Mrs. Bennet invited him to dine with them; but, with many expressions of concern, he confessed himself engaged elsewhere.

5“Next time you call,” said she, “I hope we shall be more lucky.”

6He should be particularly happy at any time, etc., etc.; and if she would give him leave, would take an early opportunity of waiting on them.

7“Can you come to-morrow?”

8Yes, he had no engagement at all for to-morrow; and her invitation was accepted with alacrity.

9He came, and in such very good time, that the ladies were none of them dressed. 10In ran Mrs. Bennet to her daughters’ room, in her dressing-gown, and with her hair half finished, crying out,—

11“My dear Jane, make haste and hurry down. 12He is come—Mr. Bingley is come. 13He is, indeed. 14Make haste, make haste. 15Here, Sarah, come to Miss Bennet this moment, and help her on with her gown. 16Never mind Miss Lizzy’s hair.”

17“We will be down as soon as we can,” said Jane; “but I dare say Kitty is forwarder than either of us, for she went upstairs half an hour ago.”

18“Oh! hang Kitty! 19what has she to do with it? 20Come, be quick, be quick! where is your sash, my dear?”

21But when her mother was gone, Jane would not be prevailed on to go down without one of her sisters.

22The same anxiety to get them by themselves was visible again in the evening. 23After tea, Mr. Bennet retired to the library, as was his custom, and Mary went upstairs to her instrument. 24Two obstacles of the five being thus removed, Mrs. Bennet sat looking and winking at Elizabeth and Catherine for a considerable time, without making any impression on them. 25Elizabeth would not observe her; and when at last Kitty did, she very innocently said, “What is the matter, mamma? 26What do you keep winking at me for? 27What am I to do?”

28“Nothing, child, nothing. 29I did not wink at you.” 30She then sat still five minutes longer; but unable to waste such a precious occasion, she suddenly got up, and saying to Kitty,—

31“Come here, my love, I want to speak to you,” took her out of the room. 32Jane instantly gave a look at Elizabeth which spoke her distress at such premeditation, and her entreaty that she would not give in to it. 33In a few minutes, Mrs. Bennet half opened the door and called out,—

34“Lizzy, my dear, I want to speak with you.”

35Elizabeth was forced to go.

36“We may as well leave them by themselves, you know,” said her mother as soon as she was in the hall. 37“Kitty and I are going upstairs to sit in my dressing-room.”

38Elizabeth made no attempt to reason with her mother, but remained quietly in the hall till she and Kitty were out of sight, then returned into the drawing-room.

39Mrs. Bennet’s schemes for this day were ineffectual. 40Bingley was everything that was charming, except the professed lover of her daughter. 41His ease and cheerfulness rendered him a most agreeable addition to their evening party; and he bore with the ill-judged officiousness of the mother, and heard all her silly remarks with a forbearance and command of countenance particularly grateful to the daughter.

42He scarcely needed an invitation to stay supper; and before he went away an engagement was formed, chiefly through his own and Mrs. Bennet’s means, for his coming next morning to shoot with her husband.

43After this day, Jane said no more of her indifference. 44Not a word passed between the sisters concerning Bingley; but Elizabeth went to bed in the happy belief that all must speedily be concluded, unless Mr. Darcy returned within the stated time. 45Seriously, however, she felt tolerably persuaded that all this must have taken place with that gentleman’s concurrence.

46Bingley was punctual to his appointment; and he and Mr. Bennet spent the morning together, as had been agreed on. 47The latter was much more agreeable than his companion expected. 48There was nothing of presumption or folly in Bingley that could provoke his ridicule, or disgust him into silence; and he was more communicative, and less eccentric, than the other had ever seen him. 49Bingley of course returned with him to dinner; and in the evening Mrs. Bennet’s invention was again at work to get everybody away from him and her daughter. 50Elizabeth, who had a letter to write, went into the breakfast-room for that purpose soon after tea; for as the others were all going to sit down to cards, she could not be wanted to counteract her mother’s schemes.

51But on her returning to the drawing-room, when her letter was finished, she saw, to her infinite surprise, there was reason to fear that her mother had been too ingenious for her. 52On opening the door, she perceived her sister and Bingley standing together over the hearth, as if engaged in earnest conversation; and had this led to no suspicion, the faces of both, as they hastily turned round and moved away from each other, would have told it all. 53Their situation was awkward enough; but hers she thought was still worse. 54Not a syllable was uttered by either; and Elizabeth was on the point of going away again, when Bingley, who as well as the other had sat down, suddenly rose, and, whispering a few words to her sister, ran out of the room.

55Jane could have no reserves from Elizabeth, where confidence would give pleasure; and, instantly embracing her, acknowledged, with the liveliest emotion, that she was the happiest creature in the world.

56“’Tis too much!” 57she added, “by far too much. 58I do not deserve it. 59Oh, why is not everybody as happy?”

61Elizabeth’s congratulations were given with a sincerity, a warmth, a delight, which words could but poorly express. 62Every sentence of kindness was a fresh source of happiness to Jane. 63But she would not allow herself to stay with her sister, or say half that remained to be said, for the present.

64“I must go instantly to my mother,” she cried. 65“I would not on any account trifle with her affectionate solicitude, or allow her to hear it from anyone but myself. 66He is gone to my father already. 67Oh, Lizzy, to know that what I have to relate will give such pleasure to all my dear family! 68how shall I bear so much happiness?”

69She then hastened away to her mother, who had purposely broken up the card-party, and was sitting upstairs with Kitty.

70Elizabeth, who was left by herself, now smiled at the rapidity and ease with which an affair was finally settled, that had given them so many previous months of suspense and vexation.

71“And this,” said she, “is the end of all his friend’s anxious circumspection! 72of all his sister’s falsehood and contrivance! 73the happiest, wisest, and most reasonable end!”

74In a few minutes she was joined by Bingley, whose conference with her father had been short and to the purpose.

75“Where is your sister?” said he hastily, as he opened the door.

76“With my mother upstairs. 77She will be down in a moment, I dare say.”

78He then shut the door, and, coming up to her, claimed the good wishes and affection of a sister. 79Elizabeth honestly and heartily expressed her delight in the prospect of their relationship. 80They shook hands with great cordiality; and then, till her sister came down, she had to listen to all he had to say of his own happiness, and of Jane’s perfections; and in spite of his being a lover, Elizabeth really believed all his expectations of felicity to be rationally founded, because they had for basis the excellent understanding and super-excellent disposition of Jane, and a general similarity of feeling and taste between her and himself.

81It was an evening of no common delight to them all; the satisfaction of Miss Bennet’s mind gave such a glow of sweet animation to her face, as made her look handsomer than ever. 82Kitty simpered and smiled, and hoped her turn was coming soon. 83Mrs. Bennet could not give her consent, or speak her approbation in terms warm enough to satisfy her feelings, though she talked to Bingley of nothing else, for half an hour; and when Mr. Bennet joined them at supper, his voice and manner plainly showed how really happy he was.

84Not a word, however, passed his lips in allusion to it, till their visitor took his leave for the night; but as soon as he was gone, he turned to his daughter and said,—

85“Jane, I congratulate you. 86You will be a very happy woman.”

87Jane went to him instantly, kissed him, and thanked him for his goodness.

88“You are a good girl,” he replied, “and I have great pleasure in thinking you will be so happily settled. 89I have not a doubt of your doing very well together. 90Your tempers are by no means unlike. 91You are each of you so complying, that nothing will ever be resolved on; so easy, that every servant will cheat you; and so generous, that you will always exceed your income.”

92“I hope not so. 93Imprudence or thoughtlessness in money matters would be unpardonable in me.”

94“Exceed their income! 95My dear Mr. Bennet,” cried his wife, “what are you talking of? 96Why, he has four or five thousand a year, and very likely more.” 97Then addressing her daughter, “Oh, my dear, dear Jane, I am so happy! 98I am sure I shan’t get a wink of sleep all night. 99I knew how it would be. 100I always said it must be so, at last. 101I was sure you could not be so beautiful for nothing! 102I remember, as soon as ever I saw him, when he first came into Hertfordshire last year, I thought how likely it was that you should come together. 103Oh, he is the handsomest young man that ever was seen!”

104Wickham, Lydia, were all forgotten. 105Jane was beyond competition her favourite child. 106At that moment she cared for no other. 107Her younger sisters soon began to make interest with her for objects of happiness which she might in future be able to dispense.

108Mary petitioned for the use of the library at Netherfield; and Kitty begged very hard for a few balls there every winter.

109Bingley, from this time, was of course a daily visitor at Longbourn; coming frequently before breakfast, and always remaining till after supper; unless when some barbarous neighbour, who could not be enough detested, had given him an invitation to dinner, which he thought himself obliged to accept.

110Elizabeth had now but little time for conversation with her sister; for while he was present Jane had no attention to bestow on anyone else: but she found herself considerably useful to both of them, in those hours of separation that must sometimes occur. 111In the absence of Jane, he always attached himself to Elizabeth for the pleasure of talking of her; and when Bingley was gone, Jane constantly sought the same means of relief.

112“He has made me so happy,” said she, one evening, “by telling me that he was totally ignorant of my being in town last spring! 113I had not believed it possible.”

114“I suspected as much,” replied Elizabeth. 115“But how did he account for it?”

116“It must have been his sisters’ doing. 117They were certainly no friends to his acquaintance with me, which I cannot wonder at, since he might have chosen so much more advantageously in many respects. 118But when they see, as I trust they will, that their brother is happy with me, they will learn to be contented, and we shall be on good terms again: though we can never be what we once were to each other.”

119“That is the most unforgiving speech,” said Elizabeth, “that I ever heard you utter. 120Good girl! 121It would vex me, indeed, to see you again the dupe of Miss Bingley’s pretended regard.”

122“Would you believe it, Lizzy, that when he went to town last November he really loved me, and nothing but a persuasion of my being indifferent would have prevented his coming down again?”

123“He made a little mistake, to be sure; but it is to the credit of his modesty.”

124This naturally introduced a panegyric from Jane on his diffidence, and the little value he put on his own good qualities.

125Elizabeth was pleased to find that he had not betrayed the interference of his friend; for, though Jane had the most generous and forgiving heart in the world, she knew it was a circumstance which must prejudice her against him.

126“I am certainly the most fortunate creature that ever existed!” cried Jane. 127“Oh, Lizzy, why am I thus singled from my family, and blessed above them all? 128If I could but see you as happy! 129If there were but such another man for you!”

130“If you were to give me forty such men I never could be so happy as you. 131Till I have your disposition, your goodness, I never can have your happiness. 132No, no, let me shift for myself; and, perhaps, if I have very good luck, I may meet with another Mr. Collins in time.”

133The situation of affairs in the Longbourn family could not be long a secret. 134Mrs. Bennet was privileged to whisper it to Mrs. Philips, and she ventured, without any permission, to do the same by all her neighbours in Meryton.

135The Bennets were speedily pronounced to be the luckiest family in the world; though only a few weeks before, when Lydia had first run away, they had been generally proved to be marked out for misfortune.
