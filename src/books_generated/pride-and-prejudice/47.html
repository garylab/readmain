1CHAPTER XLVII.

2“I HAVE been thinking it over again, Elizabeth,” said her uncle, as they drove from the town; “and really, upon serious consideration, I am much more inclined than I was to judge as your eldest sister does of the matter. 3It appears to me so very unlikely that any young man should form such a design against a girl who is by no means unprotected or friendless, and who was actually staying in his Colonel’s family, that I am strongly inclined to hope the best. 4Could he expect that her friends would not step forward? 5Could he expect to be noticed again by the regiment, after such an affront to Colonel Forster? 6His temptation is not adequate to the risk.”

7“Do you really think so?” cried Elizabeth, brightening up for a moment.

8“Upon my word,” said Mrs. Gardiner, “I begin to be of your uncle’s opinion. 9It is really too great a violation of decency, honour, and interest, for him to be guilty of it. 10I cannot think so very ill of Wickham. 11Can you, yourself, Lizzie, so wholly give him up, as to believe him capable of it?”

12“Not perhaps of neglecting his own interest. 13But of every other neglect I can believe him capable. 14If, indeed, it should be so! 15But I dare not hope it. 16Why should they not go on to Scotland, if that had been the case?”

17“In the first place,” replied Mr. Gardiner, “there is no absolute proof that they are not gone to Scotland.”

18“Oh, but their removing from the chaise into a hackney coach is such a presumption! 19And, besides, no traces of them were to be found on the Barnet road.”

20“Well, then,—supposing them to be in London—they may be there, though for the purpose of concealment, for no more exceptionable purpose. 21It is not likely that money should be very abundant on either side; and it might strike them that they could be more economically, though less expeditiously, married in London, than in Scotland.”

22“But why all this secrecy? 23Why any fear of detection? 24Why must their marriage be private? 25Oh, no, no—this is not likely. 26His most particular friend, you see by Jane’s account, was persuaded of his never intending to marry her. 27Wickham will never marry a woman without some money. 28He cannot afford it. 29And what claims has Lydia, what attractions has she beyond youth, health, and good humour, that could make him for her sake forego every chance of benefiting himself by marrying well? 30As to what restraint the apprehensions of disgrace in the corps might throw on a dishonourable elopement with her, I am not able to judge; for I know nothing of the effects that such a step might produce. 31But as to your other objection, I am afraid it will hardly hold good. 32Lydia has no brothers to step forward; and he might imagine, from my father’s behaviour, from his indolence and the little attention he has ever seemed to give to what was going forward in his family, that he would do as little and think as little about it, as any father could do, in such a matter.”

33“But can you think that Lydia is so lost to everything but love of him, as to consent to live with him on any other terms than marriage?”

34“It does seem, and it is most shocking, indeed,” replied Elizabeth, with tears in her eyes, “that a sister’s sense of decency and virtue in such a point should admit of doubt. 35But, really, I know not what to say. 36Perhaps I am not doing her justice. 37But she is very young: she has never been taught to think on serious subjects; and for the last half year, nay, for a twelvemonth, she has been given up to nothing but amusement and vanity. 38She has been allowed to dispose of her time in the most idle and frivolous manner, and to adopt any opinions that came in her way. 39Since the ——shire were first quartered in Meryton, nothing but love, flirtation, and officers, have been in her head. 40She has been doing everything in her power, by thinking and talking on the subject, to give greater—what shall I call it?—susceptibility to her feelings; which are naturally lively enough. 41And we all know that Wickham has every charm of person and address that can captivate a woman.”

42“But you see that Jane,” said her aunt, “does not think so ill of Wickham, as to believe him capable of the attempt.”

43“Of whom does Jane ever think ill? 44And who is there, whatever might be their former conduct, that she would believe capable of such an attempt, till it were proved against them? 45But Jane knows, as well as I do, what Wickham really is. 46We both know that he has been profligate in every sense of the word; that he has neither integrity nor honour; that he is as false and deceitful as he is insinuating.”

47“And do you really know all this?” cried Mrs. Gardiner, whose curiosity as to the mode of her intelligence was all alive.

48“I do, indeed,” replied Elizabeth, colouring. 49“I told you the other day of his infamous behaviour to Mr. Darcy; and you, yourself, when last at Longbourn, heard in what manner he spoke of the man who had behaved with such forbearance and liberality towards him. 50And there are other circumstances which I am not at liberty—which it is not worth while to relate; but his lies about the whole Pemberley family are endless. 51From what he said of Miss Darcy, I was thoroughly prepared to see a proud, reserved, disagreeable girl. 52Yet he knew to the contrary himself. 53He must know that she was as amiable and unpretending as we have found her.”

54“But does Lydia know nothing of this? 55can she be ignorant of what you and Jane seem so well to understand?”

56“Oh, yes!—that, that is the worst of all. 57Till I was in Kent, and saw so much both of Mr. Darcy and his relation Colonel Fitzwilliam, I was ignorant of the truth myself. 58And when I returned home the ——shire was to leave Meryton in a week or fortnight’s time. 59As that was the case, neither Jane, to whom I related the whole, nor I, thought it necessary to make our knowledge public; for of what use could it apparently be to anyone, that the good opinion, which all the neighbourhood had of him, should then be overthrown? 60And even when it was settled that Lydia should go with Mrs. Forster, the necessity of opening her eyes to his character never occurred to me. 61That she could be in any danger from the deception never entered my head. 62That such a consequence as this should ensue, you may easily believe was far enough from my thoughts.”

63“When they all removed to Brighton, therefore, you had no reason, I suppose, to believe them fond of each other?”

64“Not the slightest. 65I can remember no symptom of affection on either side; and had anything of the kind been perceptible, you must be aware that ours is not a family on which it could be thrown away. 66When first he entered the corps, she was ready enough to admire him; but so we all were. 67Every girl in or near Meryton was out of her senses about him for the first two months: but he never distinguished her by any particular attention; and, consequently, after a moderate period of extravagant and wild admiration, her fancy for him gave way, and others of the regiment, who treated her with more distinction, again became her favourites.”

68It may be easily believed, that however little of novelty could be added to their fears, hopes, and conjectures, on this interesting subject by its repeated discussion, no other could detain them from it long, during the whole of the journey. 69From Elizabeth’s thoughts it was never absent. 70Fixed there by the keenest of all anguish, self-reproach, she could find no interval of ease or forgetfulness.

71They travelled as expeditiously as possible; and sleeping one night on the road, reached Longbourn by dinnertime the next day. 72It was a comfort to Elizabeth to consider that Jane could not have been wearied by long expectations.

73The little Gardiners, attracted by the sight of a chaise, were standing on the steps of the house, as they entered the paddock; and when the carriage drove up to the door, the joyful surprise that lighted up their faces and displayed itself over their whole bodies, in a variety of capers and frisks, was the first pleasing earnest of their welcome.

74Elizabeth jumped out; and after giving each of them a hasty kiss, hurried into the vestibule, where Jane, who came running downstairs from her mother’s apartment, immediately met her.

75Elizabeth, as she affectionately embraced her, whilst tears filled the eyes of both, lost not a moment in asking whether anything had been heard of the fugitives.

76“Not yet,” replied Jane. 77“But now that my dear uncle is come, I hope everything will be well.”

78“Is my father in town?”

79“Yes, he went on Tuesday, as I wrote you word.”

80“And have you heard from him often?”

81“We have heard only once. 82He wrote me a few lines on Wednesday, to say that he had arrived in safety, and to give me his directions, which I particularly begged him to do. 83He merely added, that he should not write again, till he had something of importance to mention.”

84“And my mother—how is she? 85How are you all?”

86“My mother is tolerably well, I trust; though her spirits are greatly shaken. 87She is upstairs, and will have great satisfaction in seeing you all. 88She does not yet leave her dressing-room. 89Mary and Kitty, thank Heaven! 90are quite well.”

91“But you—how are you?” cried Elizabeth. 92“You look pale. 93How much you must have gone through!”

94Her sister, however, assured her of her being perfectly well; and their conversation, which had been passing while Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner were engaged with their children, was now put an end to by the approach of the whole party. 95Jane ran to her uncle and aunt, and welcomed and thanked them both, with alternate smiles and tears.

96When they were all in the drawing-room, the questions which Elizabeth had already asked were of course repeated by the others, and they soon found that Jane had no intelligence to give. 97The sanguine hope of good, however, which the benevolence of her heart suggested, had not yet deserted her; she still expected that it would all end well, and that every morning would bring some letter, either from Lydia or her father, to explain their proceedings, and, perhaps, announce the marriage.

98Mrs. Bennet, to whose apartment they all repaired, after a few minutes’ conversation together, received them exactly as might be expected; with tears and lamentations of regret, invectives against the villainous conduct of Wickham, and complaints of her own sufferings and ill-usage; blaming everybody but the person to whose ill-judging indulgence the errors of her daughter must be principally owing.

99“If I had been able,” said she, “to carry my point in going to Brighton with all my family, this would not have happened: but poor dear Lydia had nobody to take care of her. 100Why did the Forsters ever let her go out of their sight? 101I am sure there was some great neglect or other on their side, for she is not the kind of girl to do such a thing, if she had been well looked after. 102I always thought they were very unfit to have the charge of her; but I was over-ruled, as I always am. 103Poor, dear child! 104And now here’s Mr. Bennet gone away, and I know he will fight Wickham, wherever he meets him, and then he will be killed, and what is to become of us all? 105The Collinses will turn us out, before he is cold in his grave; and if you are not kind to us, brother, I do not know what we shall do.”

106They all exclaimed against such terrific ideas; and Mr. Gardiner, after general assurances of his affection for her and all her family, told her that he meant to be in London the very next day, and would assist Mr. Bennet in every endeavour for recovering Lydia.

107“Do not give way to useless alarm,” added he: “though it is right to be prepared for the worst, there is no occasion to look on it as certain. 108It is not quite a week since they left Brighton. 109In a few days more, we may gain some news of them; and till we know that they are not married, and have no design of marrying, do not let us give the matter over as lost. 110As soon as I get to town, I shall go to my brother, and make him come home with me to Gracechurch Street, and then we may consult together as to what is to be done.”

111“Oh, my dear brother,” replied Mrs. Bennet, “that is exactly what I could most wish for. 112And now do, when you get to town, find them out, wherever they may be; and if they are not married already, make them marry. 113And as for wedding clothes, do not let them wait for that, but tell Lydia she shall have as much money as she chooses to buy them, after they are married. 114And, above all things, keep Mr. Bennet from fighting. 115Tell him what a dreadful state I am in—that I am frightened out of my wits; and have such tremblings, such flutterings all over me, such spasms in my side, and pains in my head, and such beatings at my heart, that I can get no rest by night nor by day. 116And tell my dear Lydia not to give any directions about her clothes till she has seen me, for she does not know which are the best warehouses. 117Oh, brother, how kind you are! 118I know you will contrive it all.”

119But Mr. Gardiner, though he assured her again of his earnest endeavours in the cause, could not avoid recommending moderation to her, as well in her hopes as her fears; and after talking with her in this manner till dinner was on table, they left her to vent all her feelings on the housekeeper, who attended in the absence of her daughters.

120Though her brother and sister were persuaded that there was no real occasion for such a seclusion from the family, they did not attempt to oppose it; for they knew that she had not prudence enough to hold her tongue before the servants, while they waited at table, and judged it better that one only of the household, and the one whom they could most trust, should comprehend all her fears and solicitude on the subject.

121In the dining-room they were soon joined by Mary and Kitty, who had been too busily engaged in their separate apartments to make their appearance before. 122One came from her books, and the other from her toilette. 123The faces of both, however, were tolerably calm; and no change was visible in either, except that the loss of her favourite sister, or the anger which she had herself incurred in the business, had given something more of fretfulness than usual to the accents of Kitty. 124As for Mary, she was mistress enough of herself to whisper to Elizabeth, with a countenance of grave reflection, soon after they were seated at table,—

125“This is a most unfortunate affair, and will probably be much talked of. 126But we must stem the tide of malice, and pour into the wounded bosoms of each other the balm of sisterly consolation.”

127Then perceiving in Elizabeth no inclination of replying, she added, “Unhappy as the event must be for Lydia, we may draw from it this useful lesson:—that loss of virtue in a female is irretrievable, that one false step involves her in endless ruin, that her reputation is no less brittle than it is beautiful, and that she cannot be too much guarded in her behaviour towards the undeserving of the other sex.”

128Elizabeth lifted up her eyes in amazement, but was too much oppressed to make any reply. 129Mary, however, continued to console herself with such kind of moral extractions from the evil before them.

130In the afternoon, the two elder Miss Bennets were able to be for half an hour by themselves; and Elizabeth instantly availed herself of the opportunity of making any inquiries which Jane was equally eager to satisfy. 131After joining in general lamentations over the dreadful sequel of this event, which Elizabeth considered as all but certain, and Miss Bennet could not assert to be wholly impossible, the former continued the subject by saying, “But tell me all and everything about it which I have not already heard. 132Give me further particulars. 133What did Colonel Forster say? 134Had they no apprehension of anything before the elopement took place? 135They must have seen them together for ever.”

136“Colonel Forster did own that he had often suspected some partiality, especially on Lydia’s side, but nothing to give him any alarm. 137I am so grieved for him. 138His behaviour was attentive and kind to the utmost. 139He was coming to us, in order to assure us of his concern, before he had any idea of their not being gone to Scotland: when that apprehension first got abroad, it hastened his journey.”

140“And was Denny convinced that Wickham would not marry? 141Did he know of their intending to go off? 142Had Colonel Forster seen Denny himself?”

143“Yes; but when questioned by him, Denny denied knowing anything of their plan, and would not give his real opinion about it. 144He did not repeat his persuasion of their not marrying, and from that I am inclined to hope he might have been misunderstood before.”

145“And till Colonel Forster came himself, not one of you entertained a doubt, I suppose, of their being really married?”

146“How was it possible that such an idea should enter our brains? 147I felt a little uneasy—a little fearful of my sister’s happiness with him in marriage, because I knew that his conduct had not been always quite right. 148My father and mother knew nothing of that; they only felt how imprudent a match it must be. 149Kitty then owned, with a very natural triumph on knowing more than the rest of us, that in Lydia’s last letter she had prepared her for such a step. 150She had known, it seems, of their being in love with each other many weeks.”

151“But not before they went to Brighton?”

152“No, I believe not.”

153“And did Colonel Forster appear to think ill of Wickham himself? 154Does he know his real character?”

155“I must confess that he did not speak so well of Wickham as he formerly did. 156He believed him to be imprudent and extravagant; and since this sad affair has taken place, it is said that he left Meryton greatly in debt: 157but I hope this may be false.”

158“Oh, Jane, had we been less secret, had we told what we knew of him, this could not have happened!”

159“Perhaps it would have been better,” replied her sister.

160“But to expose the former faults of any person, without knowing what their present feelings were, seemed unjustifiable.”

161“We acted with the best intentions.”

162“Could Colonel Forster repeat the particulars of Lydia’s note to his wife?”

163“He brought it with him for us to see.”

164Jane then took it from her pocket-book, and gave it to Elizabeth. 165These were the contents:—

166“My dear Harriet,

167“You will laugh when you know where I am gone, and I cannot help laughing myself at your surprise to-morrow morning, as soon as I am missed. 168I am going to Gretna Green, and if you cannot guess with who, I shall think you a simpleton, for there is but one man in the world I love, and he is an angel. 169I should never be happy without him, so think it no harm to be off. 170You need not send them word at Longbourn of my going, if you do not like it, for it will make the surprise the greater when I write to them, and sign my name Lydia Wickham. 171What a good joke it will be! 172I can hardly write for laughing. 173Pray make my excuses to Pratt for not keeping my engagement, and dancing with him to-night. 174Tell him I hope he will excuse me when he knows all, and tell him I will dance with him at the next ball we meet with great pleasure. 175I shall send for my clothes when I get to Longbourn; but I wish you would tell Sally to mend a great slit in my worked muslin gown before they are packed up. 176Good-bye. 177Give my love to Colonel Forster. 178I hope you will drink to our good journey.

179“Your affectionate friend, “Lydia Bennet.”

181“Oh, thoughtless, thoughtless Lydia!” cried Elizabeth when she had finished it. 182“What a letter is this, to be written at such a moment! 183But at least it shows that she was serious in the object of her journey. 184Whatever he might afterwards persuade her to, it was not on her side a scheme of infamy. 185My poor father! 186how he must have felt it!”

187“I never saw anyone so shocked. 188He could not speak a word for full ten minutes. 189My mother was taken ill immediately, and the whole house in such confusion!”

190“Oh, Jane,” cried Elizabeth, “was there a servant belonging to it who did not know the whole story before the end of the day?”

191“I do not know: I hope there was. 192But to be guarded at such a time is very difficult. 193My mother was in hysterics; and though I endeavoured to give her every assistance in my power, I am afraid I did not do so much as I might have done. 194But the horror of what might possibly happen almost took from me my faculties.”

195“Your attendance upon her has been too much for you. 196You do not look well. 197Oh that I had been with you! 198you have had every care and anxiety upon yourself alone.”

199“Mary and Kitty have been very kind, and would have shared in every fatigue, I am sure, but I did not think it right for either of them. 200Kitty is slight and delicate, and Mary studies so much that her hours of repose should not be broken in on. 201My aunt Philips came to Longbourn on Tuesday, after my father went away; and was so good as to stay till Thursday with me. 202She was of great use and comfort to us all, and Lady Lucas has been very kind: she walked here on Wednesday morning to condole with us, and offered her services, or any of her daughters, if they could be of use to us.”

203“She had better have stayed at home,” cried Elizabeth: “perhaps she meant well, but, under such a misfortune as this, one cannot see too little of one’s neighbours. 204Assistance is impossible; condolence, insufferable. 205Let them triumph over us at a distance, and be satisfied.”

206She then proceeded to inquire into the measures which her father had intended to pursue, while in town, for the recovery of his daughter.

207“He meant, I believe,” replied Jane, “to go to Epsom, the place where they last changed horses, see the postilions, and try if anything could be made out from them. 208His principal object must be to discover the number of the hackney coach which took them from Clapham. 209It had come with a fare from London; and as he thought the circumstance of a gentleman and lady’s removing from one carriage into another might be remarked, he meant to make inquiries at Clapham. 210If he could anyhow discover at what house the coachman had before set down his fare, he determined to make inquiries there, and hoped it might not be impossible to find out the stand and number of the coach. 211I do not know of any other designs that he had formed; but he was in such a hurry to be gone, and his spirits so greatly discomposed, that I had difficulty in finding out even so much as this.”
